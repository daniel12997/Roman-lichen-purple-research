# Roman Lichen Purple (Orchil) Dye

**Research Resources & Scholarly Sources — Lichen-Derived Purple in the Roman World**

> **What this is:** a pointer list to published sources on lichen-derived purple dye (orchil) in the Roman and late-antique world — primary analytical chemistry, reference volumes, lichen taxonomy, ethnobotanical analogues, ancient texts, and Roman-period case studies where orchil has been chemically identified. Each entry is meant to help you decide whether to open the source and what to look for when you do.
>
> **What this isn't:** a general history of Roman purple, and it is not a research artifact to be cited directly. Shellfish (Tyrian/murex) purple appears here only as context — it is what orchil imitated, competed with, and was sometimes used to adulterate. The "Summary of Key Findings" at the bottom is rough orientation; trace any claim back to its cited paper before citing elsewhere.

The list covers orchil chemistry (the 7-hydroxy/amino-phenoxazone chromophore system elucidated by Musso and Beecken in the 1960s), modern HPLC-ESI-MS/MS methods for detecting orchil on textiles and parchment (Witkowski et al. 2017), lichen-species discrimination (distinguishing *Roccella tinctoria*, *Ochrolechia tartarea*, and *Lasallia pustulata* by mass-spectrometric fingerprint; Calà et al. 2019), Roman-period textile identifications (Vindolanda; Walton and Taylor 1983), and the centrepiece of the lichen story: Aceto et al.'s finding that late-antique purple codices — long assumed to be murex — were in most cases dyed with orchil or folium (Aceto et al. 2019). Imperial purple manuscripts used lichen dye; this is not marginal.

## Scholarly Papers (Primary Research)

Peer-reviewed (or formally preprinted) analytical and historical studies.

### Orcein chromophore chemistry (foundational)

#### Musso, H. (1960) — Orcein- und Lackmusfarbstoffe

*Planta Medica* 8(4):432–446. Foundational German-language paper employing partition chromatography to separate crude orcein and litmus into their component dyes, elucidating structures to core phenoxazone and phenoxazine scaffolds: 7-hydroxyphenoxazone (hydroxyorcein series), 7-aminophenoxazone (aminoorcein series), and 7-aminophenoxazine derivatives. Establishes the structural basis for all subsequent chemical work on orchil dyes. Paywalled (Thieme).

<https://doi.org/10.1055/s-0028-1101580>

#### Beecken, H., et al. (1961 / 2003) — Orcein and Litmus

*Angewandte Chemie* 73(20):665–673 (1961); English reprint *Biotechnic & Histochemistry* 78(6):289–302 (2003). Canonical Musso group study presenting the first comprehensive account of orcein composition through partition chromatography, identifying 14 distinct dye components and resolving their structures. Assigns orcein to two core phenoxazone types — 7-amino- and 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazones — with α/β/γ-isomers for both series, and demonstrates that litmus is a polymeric assembly of 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone units. The fundamental chemical framework for understanding orchil. Paywalled (Wiley / T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1002/ange.19610732002>

#### Wainwright, M. (2003) — Orcein, a traditional stain revealed: some introductory remarks

*Biotechnic & Histochemistry* 78(6):285–287. Editorial introducing the English translation of the Beecken–Musso 1961 paper, contextualizing this foundational work on orcein chemistry for conservation scientists. Explains orcein's complex phenoxazone structure — up to eight major and six minor components — and its batch variability, and notes orcein's pH-dependent coloration (red in acid, blue in alkali). Entry point to the Musso corpus in English. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1080/10520290410001671317>

#### Ferreira, E. S. B., Hulme, A. N., McNab, H., Quye, A. (2004) — The natural constituents of historical textile dyes

*Chemical Society Reviews* 33(6):329–336. Tutorial review of the chemical constituents of Western historical textile dyes; organized by colour class (blue/purple indigoids, red anthraquinones, yellow flavonoids, plus over-dyed composite colours). The abstract treats lichens and shellfish only as a parenthetical example of non-indigo blue/purple sources, so this article is best cited for the general dye-class framework, not as foundational orcein chemistry — the placement in the "Orcein chromophore chemistry (foundational)" subsection above is a convenience, not a precise typological claim. **Note:** the locally downloaded file at this citation is a conference paper (MODHT project) on flavonoid dyes in tapestries, not the *Chem. Soc. Rev.* article itself; readers should obtain the published article separately. RSC paywalled; the Glasgow Enlighten record reportedly carries the wrong attached file.

<https://doi.org/10.1039/b305697j>

### Orchil analytical methodology

#### Clementi, C., Miliani, C., Romani, A., Favaro, G. (2006) — In situ fluorimetry applied to orcein in solution, on silk and wool standards, and a Renaissance tapestry

*Spectrochimica Acta A* 64(4):906–912. Applies in situ fluorimetry as a non-invasive diagnostic for orchil-dyed textiles, characterizing α-hydroxyorcein and α-aminoorcein chromophores and establishing characteristic emission maxima of 585–595 nm on silk and wool reference standards and a Renaissance tapestry fragment. Documents the photolability of orchil colorants — directly relevant to assessing survival on excavated Roman textiles, where fading may render the dye undetectable by eye. Paywalled (Elsevier).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.saa.2005.08.024>

#### Doherty, B., Gabrieli, F., Clementi, C., Cardon, D., Sgamellotti, A., Brunetti, B., Miliani, C. (2014) — SERS investigation of orchil dyed wool from *Roccella tinctoria* and *Lasallia pustulata*

*J. Raman Spectroscopy* 45(9):723–729. SERS analysis of wool dyed with orchil from two historically important lichen species provides the first Raman band assignments for orcein and demonstrates species-specific spectral differences enabling discrimination between *R. tinctoria* and *L. pustulata* based on their distinct dye-precursor constituents. An important methodological reference for lichen species discrimination via vibrational spectroscopy; uses Cardon-dyed reference materials. Paywalled (Wiley).

<https://doi.org/10.1002/jrs.4543>

#### Aceto, M., Arrais, A., Marsano, F., Agostino, A., Fenoglio, G., Idone, A., Gulmini, M. (2015) — A diagnostic study on folium and orchil dyes with non-invasive and micro-destructive methods

*Spectrochimica Acta A* 142:159–168. Applies a battery of non-invasive (FT-IR, FT-Raman, FORS, spectrofluorimetry, XRF) and micro-invasive (SERS, MALDI-TOF-MS, ICP-MS) methods to orchil and folium (*Chrozophora tinctoria*) on reference samples and painted/dyed parchments. Establishes diagnostic spectral fingerprints enabling chemical distinction between the two purple dyes — FORS is identified as the most reliable non-invasive method (orchil absorptions at ~544 and ~588 nm vs. folium at ~546 and ~577 nm), with SERS and MALDI-ToF-MS providing superior micro-invasive discrimination (diagnostic peaks at 266 m/z for folium, 672 m/z for orchil). A key incidental finding: XRF and ICP-MS detect **bromine in both orchil and folium raw materials and dyes** (up to 196 mg/kg in *R. tinctoria*), challenging the assumption that bromine is a reliable marker exclusive to Tyrian purple. Applied *in situ* to identify orchil in the 9th-c. *Libri S. Augustini de Trinitate* (Vercelli) and folium in the 12th-c. *Beatus of Liébana* (Turin). The paper is the methodological foundation for Aceto's later purple-codex work (entries below). Open (IRIS UniTO AperTO preprint; Elsevier paywalled).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.saa.2015.02.001>

#### Witkowski, B., Ganeczko, M., Hryszko, H., Biesaga, M. (2017) — Identification of orcein in 14th and 15th century liturgical paraments with HPLC-ESI/MS/MS

*Microchemical Journal* 133:370–379. Analyzes medieval silk ecclesiastical vestments using HPLC-ESI-MS/MS targeted at α-, β-, and γ-hydroxy- and aminoorcein isomers as diagnostic markers. Confirms orcein as a medieval purple dye and establishes canonical MRM (multiple reaction monitoring) transitions that became foundational reference standards for subsequent orcein authentication studies. Paywalled (Elsevier).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.microc.2017.03.049>

### Lichen-species discrimination

#### Serafini, I., Lombardi, L., Reverberi, M., Ciccola, A., Calà, E., Sciubba, F., Guiso, M., Postorino, P., Aceto, M., Bianco, A. (2018) — Advanced extraction and analytical methods applied to discrimination of lichen species used for orcein dyed yarns

*Microchemical Journal* 138:447–456. First systematic attempt to develop species-fingerprinting methods capable of discriminating lichen source from finished dyed yarns, comparing *Roccella tinctoria*, *Ochrolechia tartarea*, and *Lasallia pustulata* via complementary HPTLC, SERS, and HPLC-MS on both dyebaths and dyed yarns. Addresses a critical gap in textile authentication and provides the methodological groundwork for Calà et al. (2019). Paywalled (Elsevier).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.microc.2018.01.033>

#### Calà, E., Benzi, M., Gosetti, F., Zanin, A., Gulmini, M., Idone, A., Serafini, I., Ciccola, A., Curini, R., Whitworth, I., Aceto, M. (2019) — Towards the identification of the lichen species in historical orchil dyes by HPLC-MS/MS

*Microchemical Journal* 150:104140. Develops HPLC-MS/MS methods with dual detection modes (quadrupole-ion trap and triple quadrupole) to distinguish *Lasallia pustulata*, *Ochrolechia tartarea*, and *Roccella tinctoria*, identifying 13 characteristic precursor ions and 32 compounds with diagnostic retention-time patterns. SRM transitions and principal component analysis (PCA) discriminate species in historical textiles and parchments. Validation on historical samples reveals *L. pustulata* as the lichen source in English and Central European artifacts from the medieval period onward. The most advanced species-discrimination paper in this series. Paywalled; preprint Academia.edu/116321829.

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.microc.2019.104140>

#### Lackner, S., Ferron, S., Boustie, J., Le Devehat, F., Lumbsch, H. T., Shibayama, N. (2024) — Unraveling a Historical Mystery: Identification of a Lichen Dye Source in a Fifteenth Century Medieval Tapestry

*Heritage* 7(5):112 (pp. 2370–2384). LC-qToF-MS analysis of dark brown wool yarns from the early-15th-century "Julius Caesar" panel (c. 1400–1410, Met Cloisters "Heroes" tapestries, accession 47.101.3) detected a suite of multiply chlorinated xanthones — including thiophanic acid (2,4,5,7-tetrachloronorlichexanthone) and arthothelin (2,4,5-trichloronorlichexanthone), plus additional trichlorinated and O-methoxy trichlorinated isomers — consistent with the chemosyndrome of *Lecanora sulphurata*. The authors explicitly hedge the species assignment ("it may not be possible to definitively identify the lichen source") and note that more than one species could have been mixed; the detected compounds notably lack the atranorin/chloroatranorin depsides that a fresh *L. sulphurata* extract contains, consistent with photodegradation over centuries. This is the first identification of lichen xanthones in any historic object and the first *indication* that a boiling-water method (rather than ammonia fermentation) for lichen dyes *may* have been used before the 18th century — significantly expanding the known repertoire of pre-modern lichen colorants beyond the orcein/orchil class. Open access (MDPI).

<https://doi.org/10.3390/heritage7050112>

### Experimental reconstruction and historical recipes

#### Whitworth, I., Chen, V. J., Smith, G. D. (2025) — Milking the Orchil

*Heritage* 8(7):272. Experimental reconstruction of Stockholm Papyrus recipe S109 (3rd–4th c. CE), which specifies adding goat milk to an orchil dyebath for wool. LC-DAD-MS analysis shows that milk-containing baths produce up to six-fold higher relative amounts of α-aminoorcein and α-hydroxyorcein (modest on the Portugal set; larger on the Canary and Devon sets), yielding a visually deeper, redder hue; proposed mechanisms include milk–protein (casein) complexes with specific orceins and microbially promoted cuticle degradation (evidenced by SEM) enhancing dye uptake. The Stockholm Papyrus recipe uses "Phoenician colour" as the label for the orchil-derived purple — explicitly equating lichen purple with the prestige register of Tyrian dye (the same Stockholm corpus Reinhold 1969 fn. 17 had cited, via Caley 1927, as evidence of ancient imitation-purple manufacture). **Species caveat:** the experimental lichens were *Roccella canariensis* (Lanzarote) and *Lasallia pustulata* (Devon UK, Portugal) — not Mediterranean *R. tinctoria*; extrapolation of the milk-enhancement ratios to a Roman-period *R. tinctoria* bath is untested. Directly connects a Roman-period recipe to modern orcein chemistry. Open access (MDPI).

<https://doi.org/10.3390/heritage8070272>

#### Roca, Y., Doucet, C., Boustie, J., Tchapla, A., Héron, S., Nowik, W., Michelin, L. (2026) — Unravelling the mysteries behind orcein dye, Part B

SSRN preprint 6389507. **Not yet peer-reviewed.** Systematically varies lichen species (*Roccella fuciformis* vs *R. phycopsis*), ammonia concentration, and O₂ ratios during synthesis to trace the formation pathways of β- and γ-orcein, the compounds principally responsible for purple hues. Artificial aging experiments quantify degradation rates, establishing that β- and γ-orcein are the most degradation-prone components — direct implications for why orchil purples fade rapidly on archaeological textiles and may remain below detection limits on Roman-period finds. Treat as preliminary until peer review.

<https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6389507>

### Orchil history

#### Kok, A. (1966) — A Short History of the Orchil Dyes

*The Lichenologist* 3(2):248–272. Foundational English survey tracing orchil from earliest documented uses through early modern manufacture, identifying principal dye-yielding lichen genera (*Roccella*, *Ochrolechia*, *Lasallia*), the ammonia-fermentation process converting colourless depsides to purple orcein chromophores, and orchil's commercial role as a "bottom" dye applied beneath Tyrian purple to deepen the final hue while reducing the quantity of costly shellfish dye required. Engages critically with Pliny (NH XIII.136) and Theophrastus on *phycos thalassion*, arguing that these ancient authors were describing a *Roccella*-type lichen, not a true alga — thereby grounding lichen purple dyeing in Greco-Roman antiquity. Kok (like Smith 1921) also invokes Ezekiel 27:7 ("blue and purple from the Isles of Elishah") as a biblical-era lichen attestation; Whitworth & Koren 2016 (p. 249 n. 7) reject this on Hebrew-lexical grounds (*tekhelet* and *argaman* are shellfish-dye terms), so the Ezekiel identification is contested within this bibliography's own corpus. Paywalled (CUP).

<https://doi.org/10.1017/S002428296600029X>

#### Whitworth, I., Koren, Z. C. (2016) — Orchil and Tyrian Purple: Two Centuries of Bedfords from Leeds

*Ambix* 63(3):244–267. Archive study of the Leeds Bedford family dye-manufacturing firm documenting nearly two centuries of industrial orchil production (c. 1827 onward), tracing three generations of industrial chemists who sourced lichen from the Canary Islands, Azores, and Angola. A small envelope in the archive labelled "Tyrian Purple — Dyed with the bodies of shellfish found on the African Coast" was subjected to HPLC analysis confirming brominated indigoid markers (predominantly DBI with minor MBI and indigo), establishing the most recent authenticated historical Tyrian Purple sample. The coexistence of orchil and shellfish purple in a single commercial archive mirrors the ancient market dynamic in which cheap lichen-based orchil substituted for and adulterated expensive molluscan purple. Open access (Edelstein Center mirror; T&F paywalled).

<https://doi.org/10.1080/00026980.2016.1246160>

### Roman-period textile identifications of orchil

#### Walton, P. / Taylor, G. W. (1983) — Detection and Identification of Dyes on Pre-Hadrianic Textiles from Vindolanda

*Textile History* 14(2):115–124. **The foundational identification.** Analysis of wool textiles from pre-Hadrianic Vindolanda (late 1st c. AD Roman auxiliary fort, northern British frontier) using thin-layer chromatography (TLC) and chemical spot-test methods identifies lichen orchil (Roccellaceae) and madder on several textiles — one of the earliest published identifications of lichen-based "poor person's purple" on Roman textiles. Significant discovery showing cost-effective lichen dyes in use at frontier military supply depots; the Roccellaceae identification contextualizes orchil as an accessible purple substitute within the Roman supply chain. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1179/004049683793690552>

#### Moulherat, C. & Spantidaki, Y. (2011) — Vindolanda and its textiles: Gavvo and his tosseae

In *Purpureae Vestes III*, Valencia: 69–93. The Vindolanda textile corpus — over 700 wool fragments preserved in waterlogged deposits — reveals the material culture of Roman frontier logistics: nearly two-thirds are diamond-twill construction, purple dyes are absent or extremely rare, and documented textile supply was dominated by undyed wools supplemented by regional imports (the *tosseae* rugs supplied by trader Gavvo around AD 100). Orchil's rarity even at this frontier site — which produced the only confirmed identification (Walton/Taylor 1983) — underlines that lichen purple, while cheaper than murex, was still not ubiquitous. A crucial negative case study in Roman purple distribution. Open (Academia.edu/10508372; Cloudflare may require browser).

<https://www.academia.edu/10508372>

#### De Moor, A., Vanden Berghe, I., Van Strydonck, M., Boudin, M., Fluck, C. (2010) — Radiocarbon dating and dye analysis of Roman linen tunics and dalmatics with purple-coloured design

*Archaeological Textiles Newsletter* 51: 34–47. Radiocarbon dating (IQR 293–411 cal AD; 90% probability 202–512 AD) and HPLC-DAD dye analysis of 21 Roman-period Egyptian linen garments (5 tunics, 9 dalmatics, 7 uncertain) from nine European museum collections, with 19 purple wool decoration threads sampled from 17 garments. The dominant finding: **18 of 19 samples were "false purple" — madder-family anthraquinones (alizarin, purpurin) combined with indigotin/indirubin from indigo or woad**, produced by a two-step mordant+vat dyeing process. Only **one garment** (Brussels ACO.Tx.2477, whose structural analysis is the subject of the companion article by Verhecken-Lammens in the same ATN 51 issue, pp. 31–33) yielded genuine *Hexaplex trunculus* shellfish purple (brominated indigoids, no madder). **Orchil is absent** from all reported results — no phenoxazone-class orcein markers appear in any sample. The article does not explicitly test for orchil as a negative result, and HPLC-DAD is less sensitive for orcein than HPLC-ESI-MS/MS — so the absence is meaningful but not conclusive. Contextualises the madder+indigo imitation tradition (Pliny's "false purples") and suggests orchil's use on Roman-period material was concentrated in specific contexts (parchment codices; northern frontier) rather than being widespread in Egyptian textile production. Open (ATN 51 full issue, atnfriends.com).

<https://www.researchgate.net/publication/279923666>

### Late-antique / Byzantine purple codices (orchil on parchment)

This is the centrepiece of the lichen-in-the-Roman-world story. The three studies below overturn the long-standing assumption that "purple codices" were necessarily dyed with murex Tyrian purple.

#### Aceto, M., et al. (2019) — Mythic dyes or mythic colour? New insight into the use of purple dyes on codices

*Spectrochimica Acta A* 215:133–141. Examined 22 late-antique and Byzantine purple codices using non-invasive analytical techniques (FORS, spectrofluorimetry, XRF, and optical microscopy). The major finding: Tyrian purple is largely absent from these manuscripts; the majority relied primarily on orchil and folium — cheaper, more readily available alternatives. This overturns the "mythic" association between prestigious purple codices and genuine murex-derived purple. (The bromine-based XRF discrimination used here should be read in light of Aceto et al. 2015, which documents detectable bromine at ~100–200 mg/kg in orchil raw material: bromine *absence* still excludes murex, but low levels do not automatically confirm orchil.) Patronage of the analyzed codices varies per-manuscript and should not be generalized as "imperial." Paywalled (Elsevier).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.saa.2019.01.091>

#### Aceto, M., Melo, M. J., Calà, E., Nabais, P., Araújo, R. (2020) — Identification of the purple dye on the Vienna Genesis

Chapter in Hofmann, C. (ed.), *The Vienna Genesis: Material Analysis and Conservation of a Late Antique Illuminated Manuscript on Purple Parchment* (Böhlau Verlag, pp. 103–118; CC BY 4.0 via OAPEN). The Vienna Genesis (Cod. theol. gr. 31, 6th-c. illuminated Greek biblical manuscript, Austrian National Library) was analyzed by FORS (orchil absorption maxima at ~546 and ~588 nm), spectrofluorimetry (emission ~623 nm), micro-spectrofluorimetry on aged reference samples, SERS, and handheld XRF for bromine screening. The purple dye was identified as orchil — described in the chapter as "indisputable" — and shellfish purple was excluded (XRF bromine levels incompatible with murex dyeing; on the interpretive limits of XRF bromine as a murex-vs-orchil discriminator see Aceto et al. 2015). The chapter characterizes the manuscript itself as "probably custom-made for a private owner rather than for liturgical use" — so this specific object is not straightforwardly an "imperial" artifact. Micro-spectrofluorimetry gave the closest match to an aged reference dyed with ***Lasallia pustulata***; *Roccella tinctoria* was not excluded, and SERS alone cannot distinguish between them. The companion parchment chapter (Vnouček et al., pp. 35–70) used eZooMS combined with visual analysis of hair-follicle patterns to identify all 24 folios as sheepskin (raw eZooMS data were mixed due to 1975 conservation-glue contamination). A separate chapter (Hofmann et al., pp. 201–245) used µ-XRF, FORS, Raman, and SERS on the miniatures themselves to identify seven distinct painters by pigment palette. The companion Cod. ser. n. 2804 fragment gave identical dye results (orchil). Open access (CC BY 4.0 via OAPEN / Internet Archive).

<https://library.oapen.org/handle/20.500.12657/41206>

#### Bicchieri, M., Monti, M., Piantanida, G., Sodo, A. (2014) — The Purple Codex Rossanensis: spectroscopic characterisation and first evidence of elderberry lake in a sixth-century manuscript

*Environmental Science and Pollution Research* 21(24):14146–14157. Spectroscopic characterization (FORS, micro-Raman, micro-FTIR, XRF — FORS with Gaussian deconvolution was the decisive technique for dye identification) of the Codex Purpureus Rossanensis (6th-century Byzantine illuminated Gospel, Museo Diocesano Rossano Calabria, Italy). The purple parchment was identified as orchil (from *Roccella tinctoria*) prepared with sodium carbonate, following Stockholm Papyrus recipes 131 and 123; XRF showed no bromine, ruling out Tyrian purple. The paper's headline finding is the **first reported identification of elderberry (*Sambucus nigra*) lake** — a cyanidin-based anthocyanin pigment — as the red/violet organic lake used throughout the miniatures. An identical micro-FTIR purple-parchment spectrum from the companion Codex Purpureus Sarezzanensis (5th–6th c.) suggests that codex also was not dyed with Tyrian purple as principal dye. XRF found no bromine even after one-hour acquisitions, and the bromine-based argument should be read alongside Aceto et al. 2015's finding of ~100–200 mg/kg bromine in orchil raw material — murex-level bromine signals are absent. Bicchieri 2014 *predates* the Aceto series on purple codices by five years and is the original published evidence for orchil (not shellfish) on a 6th-c. Byzantine illuminated manuscript. Open (arXiv preprint 1404.6414).

<https://doi.org/10.1007/s11356-014-3341-6>

## Reference Volumes

Standard monographs. Each is essential; check library and ILL before purchasing.

#### Cardon, D. (2007) — *Natural Dyes: Sources, Tradition, Technology and Science*

Archetype Publications, London. 778 pp. ISBN 978-1904982005. Encyclopedic monograph covering approximately 300 colorant sources across all continents, combining botanical, zoological, and chemical detail with historical recipes. Part 2 ("Dyes from lichens and fungi") covers orchil production from *Roccella tinctoria* and related species with their chemical processing; Part 3 covers Mediterranean shellfish purple. The standard encyclopedic reference for natural dye studies; cited in virtually every subsequent analytical investigation of shellfish and lichen purples. Book.

<https://archetype.co.uk/our-titles/natural-dyes/>

#### Hofenk de Graaff, J. H., Roelofs, W. G. Th., van Bommel, M. R. (2004) — *The Colourful Past: Origins, Chemistry and Identification of Natural Dyestuffs*

Archetype / Abegg-Stiftung. Standard scholarly reference for textile conservators and dye chemists, covering the origins, chemistry, and analytical identification of the major classes of historical natural dyestuffs. Each chapter pairs historical and botanical context with detailed HPLC-DAD reference data (retention times, UV-Vis spectra, characteristic marker compounds) for lichen-derived orchil/orcein alongside anthraquinones, indigoids, and flavonoids. Together with Cardon (2007), the two foundational monographs in the field. Book.

#### Huneck, S., Yoshimura, I. (1996) — *Identification of Lichen Substances*

Springer. Catalogs approximately 700 lichen secondary metabolites with structures, spectral data (UV, IR, MS, NMR), and chromatographic identification methods (TLC, HPLC). Covers lecanoric acid, erythrin, and gyrophoric acid — the key orcinol-type depsides that serve as precursors to orcein in the orchil dye process — in comprehensive detail. Standard chemical reference for identifying which lichen species contain the precursor compounds necessary for purple dye production. Book.

## Lichen Taxonomy and Biology

These papers establish the correct names and phylogenetic distribution of the orchil-producing species. Note that *Roccella fucoides* as used in all pre-2003 dye literature is now *R. phycopsis* per Tehler 2003.

#### Frezza, C., et al. (2024) — Evaluation of the antifungal and antiproliferative properties of the lichen *Roccella tinctoria* DC.

*Pharmaceutics* 16(3):331. First comprehensive phytochemical analysis of *Roccella tinctoria* collected from the central Mediterranean (Ostia Antica, near Rome) using SPME-GC-MS and column chromatography, identifying 20 volatile and 3 non-volatile constituents: erythrin, methyl orsellinate, and montagnetol. Confirms that the depside chemistry of *R. tinctoria* growing along the Italian coastline — the very lichen accessible to Roman-era producers — is anchored in erythrin and lecanoric-acid-class depsides, providing modern analytical confirmation of the precursor chemistry relevant to Roman-era dye production. Open access (MDPI).

<https://doi.org/10.3390/pharmaceutics16030331>

#### Tehler, A. (2003) — *Roccella phycopsis* the correct name for *R. fucoides*

*Taxon* 52(3):621. Short nomenclatural note establishing that *Roccella phycopsis* Ach. (Acharius 1810) is the priority name for the lichen previously and widely known as *R. fucoides* (Neck.) Vain. **Practical consequence for all dye scholarship:** all pre-2003 literature describing *R. fucoides* as a Mediterranean orchil-producing lichen refers to the species now correctly called *R. phycopsis*. Paywalled (JSTOR).

<https://doi.org/10.2307/3647467>

#### Tehler, A., Irestedt, M. (2007) — Parallel evolution of lichen growth forms in the family Roccellaceae

*Cladistics* 23(5):432–454. Multi-locus molecular phylogeny of the Roccellaceae demonstrating that the fruticose (shrubby) growth forms characteristic of orchil-bearing *Roccella* species arose independently multiple times within the family rather than from a single ancestor — a striking case of convergent morphological evolution. Provides the modern phylogenetic backbone for understanding which *Roccella* and allied species formed taxonomically coherent units accessible to Roman-period collectors. Paywalled (Wiley).

<https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1096-0031.2007.00156.x>

#### Tehler, A., Irestedt, M., Wedin, M., Ertz, D. (2009) — Origin, evolution and taxonomy of American *Roccella*

*Systematics and Biodiversity* 7(3):307–317. Molecular phylogeny showing that American *Roccella* species form a monophyletic group most closely related to Macaronesian species rather than to the principal Old World Mediterranean clade. **Biogeographic implication for Roman scholarship:** the American + Canary Islands orchilla trade that supplied European dyers from c. 1500 CE onward was inaccessible to Roman producers; Roman orchil would have been confined to Mediterranean littoral populations of *R. tinctoria* and close relatives. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1017/S1477200009990065>

#### Tehler, A., Irestedt, M., Wedin, M., Ertz, D. (2010) — The Old World *Roccella* species outside Europe and Macaronesia

*Systematics and Biodiversity* 8(2):223–246. Revises Old World *Roccella* from Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and South and East Asia using four molecular markers, delimiting eight species and describing two new ones. Establishes which orchil-bearing *Roccella* taxa were available in Old World regions that would have served as sources or trade conduits for Roman-era purple and red-purple dyes. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1080/14772001003789554>

#### Ertz, D., Tehler, A., Irestedt, M., Frisch, A., Thor, G., van den Boom, P. (2014) — A large-scale phylogenetic revision of Roccellaceae

*Fungal Diversity* 70:31–53. Large-scale two-locus phylogenetic revision of the family Roccellaceae (~341 sequences, ~180 specimens, ~114 species), finding four established genera to be paraphyletic or polyphyletic and describing eight new genera. Any use of "Roccella" or related genera as dye sources in historical or archaeochemical literature must be evaluated against this revised generic framework. Paywalled (Springer).

<https://doi.org/10.1007/s13225-014-0286-5>

## Ethnobotanical and Practitioner Literature

**Flagged** where practitioner rather than peer-reviewed. Include for process reconstruction and comparative context; cite the analytical literature for chemical claims.

#### Grierson, S. (1986) — *The Colour Cauldron*

Mill Books, Perth. Definitive English-language survey of Scottish natural-dye folk practice, covering plant, mineral, and lichen sources from antiquity through the post-medieval period. Gives particular weight to orchil-type lichen traditions of the Scottish Highlands and Hebrides, documenting the use of *Ochrolechia tartarea* (korkir/corkir) as an ammonia-fermented purple-red dye, and situates these surviving folk traditions within a broader European orchil lineage. Best single reference for northern-European lichen purple dye practice outside the Mediterranean. Co-founding document of the Dyes in History and Archaeology (DHA) symposium series. Book.

#### Grierson, S., Duff, D. G., Sinclair, R. S. (1985a) — The Colour and Fastness of Natural Dyes of the Scottish Highlands

*J. Society of Dyers and Colourists* 101(7–8):220–228. Quantitatively characterizes the colour and fastness of wool dyed with Scottish Highland plant and lichen colorants using CIELAB and Munsell notation. Key finding for Roman dye studies: lichen-derived purples (including orchil-type dyes) exhibit poor lightfastness (typically ISO Blue Wool grade 1–2), explaining why orchil purples may have faded below visual and analytical detection limits on excavated Roman textiles even when originally present in significant quantities. Paywalled (Wiley).

<https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1478-4408.1985.tb01026.x>

#### Grierson, S., Duff, D. G., Sinclair, R. S. (1985b) — Natural Dyes of the Scottish Highlands

*Textile History* 16(1):23–43. Ethnobotanical and historical survey drawing on Gaelic oral tradition, estate records, and travellers' accounts to document lichen dye species, mordanting practice, and colour ranges in Scotland. Provides an ethnohistorical analogue for reconstructing pre-industrial lichen dye workflows and demonstrates the continuity of fermentation-based purple dyeing as living craft knowledge beyond the classical period. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1179/004049685793701223>

#### Brough, S. G. (1988) — Navajo Lichen Dyes

*The Lichenologist* 20(3):279–290. Conducted 155 dye tests on 35 lichen species from the Navajo Reservation using boiling-water extraction, ammonia fermentation, and DMSO solvent extraction, correlating resulting hues with lichen secondary metabolite profiles. Methodological analogue demonstrating how extraction method shifts orcein/chromophore ratios and thus hue; useful for understanding process variability in ancient orchil production across culturally distinct contexts. Paywalled (CUP).

<https://doi.org/10.1017/S0024282988000313>

#### Casselman, K. D. (2001) — *Lichen Dyes: The New Source Book* (2nd rev. ed.)

Dover. ISBN 9780486412313. **Practitioner handbook; not peer-reviewed.** Covers dye-lichen identification, ammonia fermentation and boiling-water extraction chemistry, ecological cautions about sustainable harvesting, and an extensive historical bibliography tracing lichen dyeing from antiquity through the industrial era. Species-by-colour charts and documentation of regional traditions make it a useful cross-reference alongside analytical findings on orchil and orcein-class compounds. Book.

#### Bolton, E. M. (1960) — *Lichens for Vegetable Dyeing*

Studio Vista. **Practitioner handbook; not peer-reviewed; taxonomy and chemistry now superseded.** Mid-20th-century craft-dyer reference with hand-dyed wool swatches; chemistry is entirely pre-Musso (1960) and describes dye behaviour empirically without reference to the orcinol-oxidation pathway or orcein chromophore structures. Serves today as a historical-ethnographic document of the mid-20th-century craft-revival movement. Book.

#### Smith, A. L. (1921) — *Lichens*

Cambridge Botanical Handbooks. Public domain; Project Gutenberg 61616; Internet Archive ia:cu31924001614480. Chapter X ("Economic and Technical") traces orchil-producing lichens from classical antiquity through the industrial era: Pliny's *Phycos Thalassion* is identified with *Roccella* ("crisp leaves, used in Crete for dyeing garments"), and Theophrastus's Cretan plants used to dye wool are similarly linked to the Roccellaceae. Smith *asserts* the *Roccella* identification without detailed philological engagement; the fuller argument is in Kok 1966 and Whitworth & Koren 2016. Smith also opens her dyeing section with Ezekiel 27:7 as evidence that lichen dyeing "dates back to a remote antiquity" — Whitworth & Koren 2016 (p. 249 n. 7) reject the Ezekiel identification on Hebrew-lexical grounds. Smith further reports that "the main supply of 'weeds' came from the Levant until the fifteenth century when supplies were obtained from the Canaries" (p. 412) — geographically consistent with Roman-accessible eastern-Mediterranean *Roccella* populations. Recounts the 14th-century Florentine rediscovery of *Roccella* by Federigo, the ammonia-fermentation chemistry, and preparation routes for orchil, cudbear, litmus, and "French purple." Readers should note Smith uses pre-molecular species names: *R. fucoides* (= *R. phycopsis* per Tehler 2003), *Lecanora tartarea* (= *Ochrolechia tartarea*), *Umbilicaria pustulata* (= *Lasallia pustulata*). Use for historical background; taxonomy pre-dates molecular systematics. Open access (Internet Archive).

<https://archive.org/details/cu31924001614480>

## Primary Ancient Texts

Brief entries focused on lichen-relevant passages. Use standard Loeb editions or translations listed below.

#### Pliny the Elder — *Natural History*, trans. H. Rackham et al., Loeb Classical Library

Key lichen passage: **Book 13.135–136** (NH XIII.135–136) — Pliny distinguishes *phycos* (a shrub) from *alga* (a herbaceous sea-plant) and notes that Cretan rock-growing *phycos* was used for a purple dye. Kok (1966) argues this refers to *Roccella*-type lichen, not a true alga; Smith (1921) endorses the identification but without detailed philological engagement. **Epistemic caveat:** Pliny does not himself make the lichen-vs-alga biological identification; the *Roccella* reading is a modern philological inference, and the underlying Pliny passage is in Loeb L370 — not held in this corpus, so claims about NH XIII.136 rest on secondary literature rather than direct audit. A useful internal control: at **NH 9.131** Pliny uses *alga* unambiguously for seaweed (murex *pelagia* "fed on seaweed"), which is at least consistent with Kok's reading that *phycos* in NH 13 refers to something distinct. Related passage at **NH 26.66** (Loeb L393, also not held) gives a three-way typology of *phycos thalassion* paralleling Dioscorides IV.98, with the "curly-leaved" Cretan type used as a "ground-colour for murex" — the most direct ancient attestation that *phycos* (lichen or alga) was used as a bottom dye beneath Tyrian. Also: **Book 9.102–140** covers murex purple at length — the moral framing of luxury in the *Naturalis Historia* opens at §103 ("what connexion is there between the sea and our clothing?"), followed by the anatomy of the dye gland, the preparation process, geographical quality rankings (Tyre / Meninx / Gaetulia / Laconia per 9.127), the Cornelius Nepos price-history of fashionable shades, and the elaborate adulterating combinations that are the essential context for understanding what orchil imitated; **Book 35.44–46** treats *purpurissum* (purple painter's pigment lake made by steeping silversmithing earth in purple dye — *not* a textile dye). Internet Archive scans of out-of-copyright Loeb volumes (Books 8–11, Loeb L353; Books 33–35, Loeb L394). See the Stage 8 primary-source report in `.work/stage8/` for the full text of NH 13.135–136 and NH 26.66 with Wikisource/Attalus URLs.

<https://archive.org>

#### Dioscorides, Pedanius — *De Materia Medica*, trans. Lily Y. Beck (2005; 3rd rev. ed.)

Olms-Weidmann, *Altertumswissenschaftliche Texte und Studien* 38. Key passage: **DM IV.98** on *phykos thalassion* ("sea-plant/sea-lichen") distinguishes three varieties — broad-leafed, reddish, and curly-leafed (the Cretan type used to dye cloth) — paralleling Pliny NH 26.66. The *Roccella*-type-lichen identification is proposed in Kok 1966 and Smith 1921 primarily for Pliny and Theophrastus; whether Dioscorides's parallel passage supports the same reading is an open philological question those works do not directly adjudicate. Beck's translation is the only complete modern English rendering, based on Max Wellmann's authoritative Greek edition (1906–1914); essential primary-source reference for the *fucus*/*phycos* identification debate. Book.

## Historical and Economic Context

Brief entries. These illuminate why orchil proliferated and what its social position was relative to murex purple.

#### Wouters, J., Vanden Berghe, I., Richard, G., Breniaux, R., Cardon, D. (2008) — Dye analysis of selected textiles from three Roman sites in the Eastern Desert of Egypt

*Dyes in History and Archaeology* 21:1–16. HPLC-DAD analysis of 1st–3rd century AD textiles from praesidia Maximianon, Krokodilô, and Didymoi along the Koptos-Berenike caravan road. The headline finding is direct chemical evidence of faked Tyrian purple: many samples show madder + indigo combinations designed to imitate true murex purple (*purpura alia* / *purpura hysginum*), with no shellfish dye detected. While orchil itself is not identified here, this paper establishes the substitution economy within which orchil operated: the demand for "purple" at all price points was met by multiple chemically distinct surrogates. ## Summary of Key Findings

### Orchil chemistry

Orchil is produced from lichen depsides (chiefly erythrin, lecanoric acid, and gyrophoric acid) by ammonia fermentation: the lichen thallus is steeped in ammonia solution with aeration, converting colourless orcinol (3,5-dihydroxytoluene) to purple orcein chromophores. Musso (1960) and Beecken et al. (1961/2003) established the structural basis: orcein is a mixture of up to 14 phenoxazone-based components divided into the 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone series (α/β/γ-hydroxyorcein) and the 7-amino-2-phenoxazone series (α/β/γ-aminoorcein). Litmus is a polymeric assembly of the hydroxy series (Beecken et al. 1961/2003). The dye is pH-dependent (red in acid, blue in alkali; Wainwright 2003) and strongly photolabile, particularly the β- and γ-orcein fractions that are primarily responsible for purple hues (Roca et al. 2026 preprint). Modern HPLC-ESI-MS/MS using MRM transitions for these six target isomers is the gold-standard identification method, established by Witkowski et al. (2017).

Species-specific fingerprinting is now possible: Calà et al. (2019) identified 13 characteristic precursor ions and 32 compounds distinguishing *Lasallia pustulata*, *Ochrolechia tartarea*, and *Roccella tinctoria* by PCA of HPLC-MS/MS profiles. Serafini et al. (2018) demonstrated complementary discrimination via HPTLC and SERS. Non-invasive pre-screening via FORS (orchil reflectance maxima at ~542–546 and ~586–588 nm depending on coordinate system — Kubelka-Munk vs. log(1/R)) and spectrofluorimetry (emission at ~585–595 nm for fresh standards, ~623 nm for aged parchment; Clementi 2006; Aceto 2015, 2020) allows tentative identification before sampling. One consequential side-finding: Aceto et al. (2015) detected significant bromine (up to ~200 mg/kg) in *R. tinctoria* raw material and in finished orchil dye, so XRF-based discrimination of orchil from Tyrian purple is quantitative (concentration regime compatible with murex vs. with orchil + terrestrial background), not presence/absence. The codex-paradox XRF-no-bromine arguments below should be read in that light.

### Lichen sources

The primary Mediterranean orchil source is *Roccella* — chiefly *R. tinctoria* and what pre-2003 literature calls *R. fucoides* (= *R. phycopsis* per Tehler 2003, the priority name; all older dye-history literature must be read accordingly). Frezza et al. (2024) confirmed that *R. tinctoria* growing at Ostia Antica (near Rome) contains erythrin (a lecanoric-acid-class depside glycoside) as the dominant non-volatile constituent of cold-methanol extracts, alongside methyl orsellinate and montagnetol — the precursor chemistry for orchil production was available along the Italian coastline in antiquity. (Note: free lecanoric acid was not detected as such in Frezza et al.; erythrin hydrolyses to lecanoric acid + erythritol during the ammonia-fermentation process that produces orcein. This apparent species-chemistry tension — Zopf 1907 / Smith 1921 had assigned *lecanoric acid* to *R. tinctoria* and erythrin to *R. fuciformis* / *R. fucoides* — likely reflects early-20th-c. extraction workflows hydrolyzing erythrin to free lecanoric acid during analysis rather than a genuine chemosyndromic difference.) The Tehler phylogenetic series (2007, 2009, 2010) establishes that the American and Canary Islands *Roccella* trade that supplied 16th–20th century Europe was inaccessible to Roman producers; Roman orchil was confined to Mediterranean littoral populations (Smith 1921 p. 412 confirms a pre-15th-c. Levantine supply chain, geographically consistent with the Roman-accessible eastern Mediterranean). Northern European and Scottish practice used *Ochrolechia tartarea* (yielding cudbear and korkir) and *Lasallia pustulata* (Grierson 1986; Grierson et al. 1985b); these are the species found in medieval English and Central European artifacts by Calà et al. (2019).

The Pliny/Dioscorides *phycos thalassion* identification remains debated and is load-bearing for the survey's claim of lichen purple in Greco-Roman antiquity. Kok (1966) argues critically for the *Roccella* reading of Pliny NH XIII.136 and Theophrastus; Smith (1921) endorses the identification but without detailed philological engagement; Whitworth & Koren (2016) supply the fullest modern philological discussion (noting that the Loeb *Natural History* index attests six different plant-referents for Greek *phycos*). The underlying Pliny passage (NH XIII.136) is in Loeb L370, which is not in this corpus, so the identification rests on secondary literature rather than direct textual audit; readers relying on this argument should consult Kok 1966 and Whitworth & Koren 2016 (p. 248–249 and footnote 7) alongside the Beck Dioscorides translation. The same note (Whitworth & Koren 2016, p. 249 n. 7) **rejects** the Ezekiel 27:7 lichen-dye identification that Smith 1921 and Kok 1966 invoke — *tekhelet* and *argaman* are Hebrew shellfish-dye terms.

### Roman-period textile evidence

Walton and Taylor (1983) on pre-Hadrianic Vindolanda is the foundational Roman textile identification: TLC and spot tests identified orchil (Roccellaceae) on wool textiles from a late 1st-century AD Roman auxiliary fort. The "poor person's purple" framing captures the economic logic: orchil was accessible where murex was not (Kok 1966; Cardon 2007). (Methodological caveat: Walton & Taylor used mid-20th-century TLC + spot tests, methods much less sensitive and specific than modern HPLC-ESI-MS/MS; the identification has not been re-analyzed with current methods.) Moulherat and Spantidaki (2011) show that even at Vindolanda — the one site with a confirmed orchil identification — purple dyes were rare across the full corpus of 700+ fragments, indicating that frontier logistics did not routinely supply any purple at scale. De Moor et al. (2010) — in ATN 51 pp. 34–47, distinct from the companion Verhecken-Lammens structural piece in the same issue — found **no orchil** in a large HPLC-DAD analyzed corpus of 21 late-Roman Egyptian linen tunics and dalmatics; the purple was overwhelmingly madder+indigo "false purple" (18 of 19 sampled threads), with genuine shellfish purple present in only one garment (Brussels ACO.Tx.2477). This pattern matches the 1st–3rd c. AD Eastern Desert findings of Wouters et al. (2008), giving a consistent picture across the 1st–5th c. that the dominant non-murex Roman-Egyptian purple was madder+indigo, not orchil. Orchil's footprint on Roman-period textile assemblages is real but sparse — which is expected given the dye's extreme photolability (Grierson et al. 1985a; Roca et al. 2026) and HPLC-DAD's lower sensitivity for orcein compared with HPLC-ESI-MS/MS.

### The purple codex paradox

The most consequential finding in recent lichen-dye scholarship is that late-antique "purple codices" — long assumed to represent the apex of murex-dyed luxury — were in most cases dyed with orchil or folium, not shellfish purple. Bicchieri et al. (2014) first identified the Codex Rossanensis parchment as orchil (from *Roccella tinctoria*) prepared with sodium carbonate (following Stockholm Papyrus recipes 131 and 123), with no XRF-detectable bromine, and proposed the same identification tentatively for the companion Codex Purpureus Sarezzanensis on the basis of identical micro-FTIR parchment spectra; Aceto et al. (2019) surveyed 22 purple codices and found Tyrian purple largely absent across the corpus; Aceto et al. (2020) identified the Vienna Genesis purple as orchil, with micro-spectrofluorimetry giving the closest match to *Lasallia pustulata* (*Roccella tinctoria* not excluded) and handheld XRF showing bromine incompatible with shellfish dyeing. **Note:** all three studies rely on XRF-no-bromine to exclude murex; Aceto et al. (2015)'s finding of ~100–200 mg/kg bromine in orchil raw material means the logic is unidirectional — bromine *absence* (or very low levels) excludes murex, but quantitative comparison against the orchil-plus-terrestrial background is now the proper discriminator, not strict presence/absence. This reframes the Byzantine-codex literature: prestige purple parchment at the top end of manuscript production was routinely dyed with lichen, not murex. The specific social register varies — the Aceto 2020 chapter describes the Vienna Genesis as "probably custom-made for a private owner rather than for liturgical use" — so claims about imperial patronage of orchil should be made per-manuscript rather than as a blanket generalization. Reinhold's (1969, 1970) framework of purple-as-status-symbol anticipated cheap imitation purples at lower social registers (Reinhold 1969 fn. 17 already cites Caley's 1927 edition of the same Stockholm Papyrus that Whitworth 2025 later reconstructs); the new finding is that the *prestige* end of the purple economy also embraced lichen dye in its most celebrated parchment artifacts. Note that this paradox concerns **parchment** — a substrate distinct from the **wool** Pliny and Dioscorides discuss for *phycos/fucus* textile dyeing and the **pigment** Pliny describes at NH 35.44–46 for *purpurissum*; orchil's presence on parchment is analytically independent of its claimed presence in Roman-era textiles.

### Late-Roman and Byzantine recipe evidence

Whitworth et al. (2025) directly reconstruct Stockholm Papyrus recipe S109 (3rd–4th c. CE), which calls for goat milk in the orchil dyebath and labels the result "Phoenician colour" — an explicit equation of lichen purple with the prestige register of Tyrian dye. The milk increases α-aminoorcein and α-hydroxyorcein uptake several-fold, yielding a deeper, redder hue. Kok (1966) traces orchil continuity from the Florentine medieval rediscovery onward; Whitworth and Koren (2016) document industrial continuity through the Leeds Bedford firm into the 20th century, showing that the orchil/murex commercial partnership documented in antiquity (Kok 1966; Cardon 2007) persisted into modern chemistry.

### Preservation and detection challenges

Orchil chromophores photodegrade severely: β- and γ-orcein, the purple-hue components, are the most labile (Roca et al. 2026). Grierson et al. (1985a) measured ISO Blue Wool lightfastness grades of 1–2 for lichen purples — extremely poor. This means many Roman-period lichen purples have faded beyond visual and analytical detection even when originally present in quantity; the Vindolanda identification depended on survival in waterlogged, anaerobic conditions atypical of most Roman sites (Moulherat and Spantidaki 2011). Non-invasive FORS and spectrofluorimetry (Clementi 2006; Aceto 2015) are now enabling tentative identifications before sampling, improving the odds of future detections.

## Terminology Glossary

Selected terms drawn from the stage-4 summary files across the orchil corpus.

**Orcein** — the crude mixture of purple-red phenoxazone-based pigments produced from orchil lichens by ammonia fermentation; up to 14 separable components (Beecken/Musso).

**Orchil (archil, orseille)** — the finished purple-red textile dye derived from *Roccella* and related lichens by ammonia fermentation; the term encompasses the dye liquor, paste, or dried powder.

**α/β/γ-Hydroxyorcein** — the three structural isomers of the 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone series within orcein; diagnostic markers for HPLC-MS/MS identification (Witkowski et al. 2017).

**α/β/γ-Aminoorcein** — the three structural isomers of the 7-amino-2-phenoxazone series within orcein; together with the hydroxy series constitute the primary analytical fingerprint (Witkowski et al. 2017).

**7-Hydroxy-2-phenoxazone** — core chromophore scaffold of the hydroxyorcein series and of litmus; the fundamental ring system elucidated by Musso (1960).

**7-Amino-2-phenoxazone** — core chromophore scaffold of the aminoorcein series; alternative phenoxazone framework within orcein.

**Lecanoric acid** — orcinol depside (orcinol + orsellinic acid ester); a principal depside in *Roccella* and related lichens (Huneck and Yoshimura 1996), hydrolysed to orcinol during orchil fermentation.

**Erythrin** — orcinol depside glycoside found prominently in *Roccella tinctoria* (confirmed by Frezza et al. 2024 at Ostia Antica); on hydrolysis yields erythritol and lecanoric acid, precursors to orcein.

**Gyrophoric acid** — orcinol tridepside abundant in many lichen species (Huneck and Yoshimura 1996); a further precursor class for orcein formation.

**Depside** — natural ester formed between two phenolic acid units (here orsellinic acid derivatives); the characteristic secondary-metabolite class of orchil lichens (Huneck and Yoshimura 1996), colourless until fermented.

**Orcinol (3,5-dihydroxytoluene)** — the key phenolic monomer released by hydrolysis of depsides; oxidised and ammoniated to yield orcein chromophores (Musso 1960; Beecken et al. 1961/2003).

***Roccella tinctoria*** — the primary Mediterranean orchil lichen; fruticose, maritime rock species; historically harvested from Italian and Iberian coasts, Macaronesia, and North Africa (Kok 1966; Cardon 2007; Frezza et al. 2024).

***R. phycopsis* (= *R. fucoides*)** — second major Mediterranean orchil lichen; *phycopsis* is the correct name per Tehler (2003); all pre-2003 literature using *fucoides* refers to this species.

***R. fuciformis*** — a further *Roccella* species used in orcein synthesis experiments (Roca et al. 2026).

***Ochrolechia tartarea*** — crustose rock lichen; Northern European orchil/cudbear source; yields a bluer hue than *Roccella*; used in Scotland as korkir/corkir (Grierson 1986; Grierson et al. 1985b).

***Lasallia pustulata*** — foliose lichen; secondary orchil source; identified as the lichen source in the Vienna Genesis (Aceto et al. 2020) and in English/Central European medieval artifacts (Calà et al. 2019).

***Lecanora sulphurata*** — Mediterranean crustose lichen producing chlorinated xanthones rather than orcein; consistent with the chemosyndrome that Lackner et al. (2024) detected on the early-15th-c. Met Cloisters *Julius Caesar* tapestry (a methodological precedent for non-orcein lichen dyes on historical textiles, not a Roman-period identification). Species assignment is hedged in the original paper; the detected compounds lacked the atranorin/chloroatranorin depsides of fresh *L. sulphurata* (photodegradation).

**Litmus** — a polymeric assembly of 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone units derived from the same orchil lichens (Beecken et al. 1961/2003); pH indicator; blue in alkali, red in acid; produced in the Netherlands from the Middle Ages (Kok 1966).

**Cudbear (korkir)** — dried, powdered orchil-type dye from *Ochrolechia tartarea*, patented by Cuthbert Gordon in Scotland c. 1758 (Kok 1966); the Scottish Gaelic equivalent of orchil (Grierson 1986).

**Folium** — purple dye from *Chrozophora tinctoria* (a plant, not a lichen); documented alongside orchil in purple codices by Aceto et al. (2019); previously of undetermined composition.

**Tyrian purple (for contrast)** — shellfish-derived purple from hypobranchial glands of *Hexaplex trunculus*, *Bolinus brandaris*, and *Stramonita haemastoma*; chromophore 6,6'-dibromoindigo (DBI); identified by HPLC via presence of brominated indigoids (Whitworth and Koren 2016).

**Argaman (for contrast)** — Biblical Hebrew term for reddish-purple, associated with molluscan dye; distinguished from *tekhelet* (blue-purple) (Whitworth and Koren 2016).

***Purpura alia* / *purpura hysginum*** — Roman terms for "other purple" / "woad-purple" (Latin); used in ancient sources and by modern scholars as a label for non-murex purple substitutes; the chemical equivalents of which include orchil (Walton and Taylor 1983), madder+indigo combinations (Wouters et al. 2008; De Moor et al. 2010), and folium (Aceto et al. 2019).

***Phycos thalassion* / *fucus*** — Greek and Latin terms used by Dioscorides and Pliny for a coastal plant or lichen used in purple dyeing; debated identification with *Roccella*-type lichens (Kok 1966, Smith 1921).

**HPLC-ESI-MS/MS** — high-performance liquid chromatography with electrospray ionization and tandem mass spectrometry; gold-standard analytical technique for orcein identification (Witkowski et al. 2017) and lichen-species discrimination (Calà et al. 2019).

**FORS (fibre optic reflectance spectroscopy)** — non-invasive UV-visible diffuse reflectance technique; orchil shows characteristic absorption maxima at ~542 and ~586 nm (Clementi 2006; Aceto et al. 2015, 2020); enables tentative identification before sampling.

**SERS (surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy)** — Raman microscopy on silver colloid particles; provides molecular fingerprints of orchil chromophores and enables species discrimination (Doherty et al. 2014).

**MRM transitions (multiple reaction monitoring)** — targeted mass spectrometry transitions (parent → product ion mass pairs) used to identify and quantify specific orcein isomers in historical textiles (Witkowski et al. 2017).

**Ammonia fermentation method (AFM)** — the traditional orchil process: lichen thallus steeped in ammonia solution with aeration, converting depsides to orcein; contrasted with the boiling-water method (BWM) for xanthone-yielding lichens (Lackner et al. 2024).
