# Roman Lichen Purple (Orchil) Dye

**Research Resources & Scholarly Sources — Lichen-Derived Purple in the Roman World**

> **What this is:** a pointer list to published sources on lichen-derived purple dye (orchil) in the Roman and late-antique world — primary analytical chemistry, reference volumes, lichen taxonomy, ethnobotanical analogues, ancient texts, and Roman-period case studies where orchil has been chemically identified. Each entry is meant to help you decide whether to open the source and what to look for when you do.
>
> **What this isn't:** a general history of Roman purple, and it is not a research artifact to be cited directly. Shellfish (Tyrian/murex) purple appears here only as context — it is what orchil imitated, competed with, and was sometimes used to adulterate. The "Summary of Key Findings" at the bottom is rough orientation; trace any claim back to its cited paper before citing elsewhere.

The list covers orchil chemistry (the 7-hydroxy/amino-phenoxazone chromophore system elucidated by Musso and Beecken in the 1960s), modern HPLC-ESI-MS/MS methods for detecting orchil on textiles and parchment (Witkowski et al. 2017), lichen-species discrimination (distinguishing *Roccella tinctoria*, *Ochrolechia tartarea*, and *Lasallia pustulata* by mass-spectrometric fingerprint; Calà et al. 2019), Roman-period textile identifications (Vindolanda; Taylor 1983), and the centrepiece of the lichen story: Aceto et al.'s finding that late-antique purple codices — long assumed to be murex — were in most cases dyed with orchil or folium (Aceto et al. 2019). Prestige purple manuscripts across the late-antique and Byzantine corpus used lichen dye; claims about the specific social register (imperial, liturgical, or private-patron) should be made per-manuscript.

## Scholarly Papers (Primary Research)

Peer-reviewed (or formally preprinted) analytical and historical studies.

### Orcein chromophore chemistry (foundational)

#### Musso, H. (1960) — Orcein- und Lackmusfarbstoffe

*Planta Medica* 8(4):432–446. German-language conference-lecture overview (Vortrag, Congresso de Cori Naturali 1960) employing partition chromatography to separate crude orcein and litmus into their component dyes, elucidating structures to core phenoxazone and phenoxazine scaffolds: 7-hydroxyphenoxazone (hydroxyorcein series), 7-aminophenoxazone (aminoorcein series), and 7-aminophenoxazine derivatives. Previews and announces the structural basis for subsequent chemical work on orchil dyes; the full peer-reviewed account of this programme is Beecken et al. (1961), cited "in press" throughout. Paywalled (Thieme).

<https://doi.org/10.1055/s-0028-1101580>

#### Beecken, H., et al. (1961 / 2003) — Orcein and Litmus

*Angewandte Chemie* 73(20):665–673 (1961); English translation *Biotechnic & Histochemistry* 78(6):289–302 (2003). The principal published account of the Musso-group work on orcein composition through partition chromatography, identifying 14 characterised components (8 major, 6 minor per Table 1) and resolving their structures. Assigns orcein to three chromophore scaffold classes: 7-amino-2-phenoxazone (aminoorceins α/β/γ), 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone (hydroxyorceins α/β/γ), and 7-aminophenoxazine (aminoorceimines α/β/γ) — and demonstrates that litmus is a polymeric assembly of 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone units. The fundamental chemical framework for understanding orchil. The 2003 entry is an English-language translation of the 1961 German paper and adds no new chemistry. Paywalled (Wiley / T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1002/ange.19610732002>

#### Wainwright, M. (2003) — Orcein, a traditional stain revealed: some introductory remarks

*Biotechnic & Histochemistry* 78(6):285–287. Three-page editorial introducing the English translation of the Beecken–Musso 1961 paper, contextualizing this foundational work on orcein chemistry for histopathologists and biological-staining researchers. Notes orcein's batch variability ("this may be due to variations in preparative procedures"), attributes more than a dozen orcein components to the Musso-group chromatographic work, and frames Beecken et al. (1961) as "undoubtedly the best summary" of that corpus. Also serves as a bridge between histological staining and craft-dyeing traditions by referencing Grierson's *Colour Cauldron* (1986). The specific decomposition into eight major and six minor components, and the pH-dependent red/deep-violet coloration, belong to the Beecken 1961/2003 paper itself (Table 1), not to Wainwright's editorial. Entry point to the Musso corpus in English. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1080/10520290410001671317>

#### Ferreira, E. S. B., Hulme, A. N., McNab, H., Quye, A. (2004) — The natural constituents of historical textile dyes

*Chemical Society Reviews* 33(6):329–336. Tutorial review of the chemical constituents of Western historical textile dyes; organized by colour class (blue/purple indigoids, red anthraquinones, yellow flavonoids, plus over-dyed composite colours). The abstract treats lichens and shellfish only as a parenthetical example of non-indigo blue/purple sources, so this article is best cited for the general dye-class framework, not as foundational orcein chemistry — the placement in the "Orcein chromophore chemistry (foundational)" subsection above is a convenience, not a precise typological claim. Open via user-provided correct file 2026-04-22; RSC paywalled.

<https://doi.org/10.1039/b305697j>

### Orchil analytical methodology

#### Clementi, C., Miliani, C., Romani, A., Favaro, G. (2006) — In situ fluorimetry applied to orcein in solution, on silk and wool standards, and a Renaissance tapestry

*Spectrochimica Acta A* 64(4):906–912. Applies in situ fluorimetry as a non-invasive diagnostic for orchil-dyed textiles, characterizing orcein chromophores and establishing characteristic emission maxima of silk ~640 nm and wool ~650 nm on reference standards and a Renaissance tapestry fragment (a marked bathochromic shift from solution-phase values of 580–595 nm). Documents the photolability of orchil colorants — directly relevant to assessing survival on excavated Roman textiles, where fading may render the dye undetectable by eye. Paywalled (Elsevier).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.saa.2005.08.024>

#### Doherty, B., Gabrieli, F., Clementi, C., Cardon, D., Sgamellotti, A., Brunetti, B., Miliani, C. (2014) — SERS investigation of orchil dyed wool from *Roccella tinctoria* and *Lasallia pustulata*

*J. Raman Spectroscopy* 45(9):723–729. SERS analysis of wool dyed with orchil from two historically important lichen species provides tentative SERS band assignments for orcein on wool at 532 nm excitation (building on prior SERS work by Leona et al. 2006 at 785 nm and FT-Raman assignments by Zaffino et al. 2014 at 1064 nm) and suggests species-specific spectral differences that may support discrimination between *R. tinctoria* and *L. pustulata* — the paper's own language is consistently hedged ("possibly," "conceivably," "seemingly," "not sufficient for confirmation"). An important methodological reference for lichen species discrimination via vibrational spectroscopy; uses Cardon-dyed reference materials. Paywalled (Wiley).

<https://doi.org/10.1002/jrs.4543>

#### Aceto, M., Arrais, A., Marsano, F., Agostino, A., Fenoglio, G., Idone, A., Gulmini, M. (2015) — A diagnostic study on folium and orchil dyes with non-invasive and micro-destructive methods

*Spectrochimica Acta A* 142:159–168. Applies a battery of non-invasive (FT-IR, FT-Raman, FORS, spectrofluorimetry, XRF) and micro-invasive (SERS, MALDI-TOF-MS, ICP-MS) methods to orchil and folium (*Chrozophora tinctoria*) on reference samples and painted/dyed parchments. Establishes diagnostic spectral fingerprints enabling chemical distinction between the two purple dyes — FORS is identified as the most reliable non-invasive method (orchil absorptions at ~544 and ~588 nm vs. folium at ~546 and ~577 nm), with SERS and MALDI-ToF-MS providing superior micro-invasive discrimination (diagnostic peaks at 266 m/z for folium, 672 m/z for orchil). A key incidental finding: XRF and ICP-MS detect **bromine in both orchil and folium raw materials and dyes** (up to 196 mg/kg in *R. tinctoria*), challenging the assumption that bromine is a reliable marker exclusive to Tyrian purple. Applied *in situ* to identify orchil in the 9th-c. *Libri S. Augustini de Trinitate* (Vercelli) and folium in the 12th-c. *Beatus of Liébana* (Turin). The paper is the methodological foundation for Aceto's later purple-codex work (entries below). Open (IRIS UniTO AperTO preprint; Elsevier paywalled).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.saa.2015.02.001>

#### Witkowski, B., Ganeczko, M., Hryszko, H., Stachurska, M., Gierczak, T., Biesaga, M. (2017) — Identification of orcein in 14th and 15th century liturgical paraments with HPLC-ESI/MS/MS

*Microchemical Journal* 133:370–379. Analyzes medieval silk ecclesiastical vestments using HPLC-ESI-MS/MS targeted at α-, β-, and γ-hydroxy- and aminoorcein isomers as diagnostic markers. Confirms orcein as a medieval purple dye and presents the first LC/ESI-MS/MS method in SRM (selected reaction monitoring) mode covering all eight major orcein components, with transitions confirmed by parallel HR-MS; quantitative parameters are approximate because dye powder (not purified standards) was used as calibrant. Paywalled (Elsevier).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.microc.2017.03.049>

### Lichen-species discrimination

#### Serafini, I., Lombardi, L., Reverberi, M., Ciccola, A., Calà, E., Sciubba, F., Guiso, M., Postorino, P., Aceto, M., Bianco, A. (2018) — Advanced extraction and analytical methods applied to discrimination of lichen species used for orcein dyed yarns

*Microchemical Journal* 138:447–456. Extends earlier fibre-discrimination work (cf. Doherty et al. 2014) with a comparative multi-lichen HPTLC/SERS/HPLC-MS dataset on both dyebaths and dyed yarns, covering three species — *Roccella tinctoria*, *Ochrolechia tartarea*, and *Lasallia pustulata* — where Doherty et al. had covered two; the paper's own title and repeated framing describe results as "preliminary." Addresses a critical gap in textile authentication and provides the methodological groundwork for Calà et al. (2019). Paywalled (Elsevier).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.microc.2018.01.033>

#### Calà, E., Benzi, M., Gosetti, F., Zanin, A., Gulmini, M., Idone, A., Serafini, I., Ciccola, A., Curini, R., Whitworth, I., Aceto, M. (2019) — Towards the identification of the lichen species in historical orchil dyes by HPLC-MS/MS

*Microchemical Journal* 150:104140. Develops HPLC-MS/MS methods with dual detection modes (quadrupole-ion trap and triple quadrupole) to distinguish *Lasallia pustulata*, *Ochrolechia tartarea*, and *Roccella tinctoria*, identifying 13 characteristic precursor ions and 32 compounds with diagnostic retention-time patterns. SRM transitions and principal component analysis (PCA) discriminate species in historical textiles and parchments. Validation on five historical samples assigned *L. pustulata* to two: the Wellcome MS.8932 English wool thread (c. 1420) and the Privilegium maius Austrian charter silk thread (1358–1359). **Three other samples — the Codex Brixianus, Vienna Genesis, and Vienna Cod. 2804 — matched *none* of the three target species**, leading the authors to hypothesise "a different, at present unidentified, lichen species … possibly a species which may no longer exist … or may have become entirely extinct." This non-match is load-bearing against any blanket "Vienna Genesis = *L. pustulata*" claim: the Aceto 2020 micro-spectrofluorimetry reading (closest to *L. pustulata*) and Calà 2019's HPLC-MS/MS reading (no match to any of *L. pustulata*, *O. tartarea*, *R. tinctoria*) are in tension. PCA of PC1 vs. PC2 accounts for 42.76% of total variance — three-species discrimination is statistically real but not overwhelming. The paper in this series with the most detailed HPLC-MS/MS species-discrimination methodology; its authors describe results as preliminary ("Towards the identification…" in the title; "preliminary results" in the Conclusions). Paywalled; preprint Academia.edu/116321829.

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.microc.2019.104140>

#### Lackner, S., Ferron, S., Boustie, J., Le Devehat, F., Lumbsch, H. T., Shibayama, N. (2024) — Unraveling a Historical Mystery: Identification of a Lichen Dye Source in a Fifteenth Century Medieval Tapestry

*Heritage* 7(5):112 (pp. 2370–2384). LC-qToF-MS analysis of dark brown wool yarns from the early-15th-century "Julius Caesar" panel (c. 1400–1410, Met Cloisters "Heroes" tapestries, accession 47.101.3) detected a suite of multiply chlorinated xanthones — including thiophanic acid (2,4,5,7-tetrachloronorlichexanthone) and arthothelin (2,4,5-trichloronorlichexanthone), plus additional trichlorinated and O-methoxy trichlorinated isomers — consistent with the chemosyndrome of *Lecanora sulphurata*. The authors explicitly hedge the species assignment ("it may not be possible to definitively identify the lichen source") and note that more than one species could have been mixed; the detected compounds notably lack the atranorin/chloroatranorin depsides that a fresh *L. sulphurata* extract contains, consistent with photodegradation over centuries. This is the first identification of lichen xanthones in any historic object and the first *indication* that a boiling-water method (rather than ammonia fermentation) for lichen dyes *may* have been used before the 18th century — significantly expanding the known repertoire of pre-modern lichen colorants beyond the orcein/orchil class. Open access (MDPI).

<https://doi.org/10.3390/heritage7050112>

### Experimental reconstruction and historical recipes

#### Whitworth, I., Chen, V. J., Smith, G. D. (2025) — Milking the Orchil

*Heritage* 8(7):272. Experimental reconstruction of Stockholm Papyrus recipe S109 (3rd–4th c. CE), which specifies adding goat milk to an orchil dyebath for wool. LC-DAD-MS analysis shows that milk-containing baths produce up to six-fold higher relative amounts of α-aminoorcein and α-hydroxyorcein (modest on the Portugal set; larger on the Canary and Devon sets), yielding a visually deeper, redder hue; proposed mechanisms include milk–protein (casein) complexes with specific orceins and microbially promoted cuticle degradation (evidenced by SEM) enhancing dye uptake. The Stockholm Papyrus recipe uses "Phoenician colour" as the label for the orchil-derived purple — explicitly equating lichen purple with the prestige register of Tyrian dye (the same Stockholm corpus Reinhold 1969 fn. 17 had cited, via Caley 1927/1932, as evidence of ancient imitation-purple manufacture — Caley's Stockholm Papyrus translation appeared in *J. Chem. Educ.* 4:979, 1927; the Jensen 2008 compilation used by Whitworth 2025 cites a revised 1932 edition). **Species caveat:** the experimental lichens were *Roccella canariensis* (Lanzarote) and *Lasallia pustulata* (Devon UK, Portugal) — not Mediterranean *R. tinctoria*; extrapolation of the milk-enhancement ratios to a Roman-period *R. tinctoria* bath is untested. Directly connects a Roman-period recipe to modern orcein chemistry. Open access (MDPI).

<https://doi.org/10.3390/heritage8070272>

#### Roca, Y., Doucet, C., Boustie, J., Tchapla, A., Héron, S., Nowik, W., Michelin, L. (2026) — Unravelling the mysteries behind orcein dye, Part B

SSRN preprint 6389507. **Not yet peer-reviewed.** Systematically varies lichen species (*Roccella fuciformis* vs *R. phycopsis*), ammonia concentration, and O₂ ratios during synthesis to trace the formation pathways of β- and γ-orcein, the compounds principally responsible for purple hues. Artificial aging experiments demonstrate photolability of β- and γ-orcein fractions under accelerated xenon-arc light exposure — peak-area decline is shown graphically (Figure 6C) without rate constants or fitted kinetics — establishing that β- and γ-orcein are the most degradation-prone components. Direct implications for why orchil purples fade rapidly on archaeological textiles and may remain below detection limits on Roman-period finds. Treat as preliminary until peer review (preprint, not peer-reviewed).

<https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6389507>

### Orchil history

#### Kok, A. (1966) — A Short History of the Orchil Dyes

*The Lichenologist* 3(2):248–272. Foundational English survey tracing orchil from earliest documented uses through early modern manufacture, identifying principal dye-yielding lichen genera (*Roccella*, *Ochrolechia*, *Lasallia*), the ammonia-fermentation process converting colourless depsides to purple orcein chromophores, and orchil's commercial role as a "bottom" dye applied beneath Tyrian purple to deepen the final hue while reducing the quantity of costly shellfish dye required. Endorses the reading of Pliny (NH XIII.136) and Theophrastus's *phycos thalassion* as a *Roccella*-type lichen rather than a true alga — inherited from Forbes (1956) and Smith (1921), and presented by Kok as "a plant which answers to the description of a lichen" rather than as a newly-argued philological case. The most detailed bibliographical treatment of *phycos* in the sources cited here is Whitworth & Koren (2016, pp. 248–249 with fn. 6 on the six plant-referents of Greek *phykos*). Kok (like Smith 1921) also invokes Ezekiel 27:7 ("blue and purple from the Isles of Elishah") as a biblical-era lichen attestation; Whitworth & Koren 2016 (p. 249 n. 7) reject this on Hebrew-lexical grounds (*tekhelet* and *argaman* are shellfish-dye terms), so the Ezekiel identification is contested within this bibliography's own corpus. Paywalled (CUP).

<https://doi.org/10.1017/S002428296600029X>

#### Whitworth, I., Koren, Z. C. (2016) — Orchil and Tyrian Purple: Two Centuries of Bedfords from Leeds

*Ambix* 63(3):244–267. Archive study of the Leeds Bedford family dye-manufacturing firm documenting nearly two centuries of industrial orchil production (c. 1827 onward), tracing three generations of industrial chemists who sourced lichen from the Canary Islands, Azores, Madeira, and Cape Verde in the early phase, and later from Angola, South America, Ceylon, and Madagascar. A small envelope in the archive labelled "Tyrian Purple — Dyed with the bodies of shellfish found on the African Coast" was subjected to HPLC analysis confirming brominated indigoid markers (predominantly DBI with minor MBI and indigo), establishing what the paper describes as "the most modern historic sample of genuine Tyrian Purple yet found." The coexistence of orchil and shellfish purple in a single commercial archive reflects the Bedfords' scientific interest in the purple of the ancients; the paper explicitly states that "molluscan purple had no direct connection to the lichen trade or other products manufactured by the Bedfords" (p. 266). Open access (Edelstein Center mirror; T&F paywalled).

<https://doi.org/10.1080/00026980.2016.1246160>

### Roman-period textile identifications of orchil

#### Taylor, G. W. (1983) — Detection and Identification of Dyes on Pre-Hadrianic Textiles from Vindolanda

*Textile History* 14(2):115–124. **The foundational identification — single-sample and tentative.** Single-author paper by G. W. Taylor (Penelope Walton appears only in the Acknowledgements; earlier drafts and citation databases that list her as co-author are in error). Analysis of 89 samples from 53 pre-Hadrianic Vindolanda wool textiles (AD 85–115; late 1st-c. Roman auxiliary fort, northern British frontier) using **UV-visible absorption spectrophotometry (Perkin-Elmer 402) on pyridine/water extracts with magnesium-acetate and pH-shift diagnostics, supplemented by paper chromatography on selected samples**. Nine samples (representing nine fabrics) gave definite dye identifications; eight of these were madder-family reds. One sample — **10/53 — was tentatively identified as a lichen purple resembling orchil**, on the basis of spectral similarity to a 19th-century orchil-dyed Spanish wool reference. Taylor's own wording is heavily hedged ("has not been positively identified," "may be the Roman orchil," "might have been a lichen dye such as the Roman dye orchil"). His UV-vis method cannot taxonomically discriminate *Roccella*, *Ochrolechia*, or *Umbilicaria*/*Lasallia* references, which all gave "to all intents and purposes identical" spectra; he also raises *Evernia prunastri* (Parmeliaceae) as a possible local alternative. The paper is therefore a single-sample, method-limited, family-agnostic tentative identification of lichen purple — not a corpus-level confirmation of orchil from *Roccellaceae*. Cardon (2007) supplies the "poor person's purple" framing; the phrase itself does not appear in Taylor or in Kok (1966). Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1179/004049683793690552>

#### Wild, J. P. (2011) — Vindolanda and its textiles: Gavvo and his tosseae

In *Purpureae Vestes III*, Valencia: 69–73. **Single-author paper by John Peter Wild** (the previous citation of this paper to "Moulherat & Spantidaki 2011 pp. 69–93" propagated an authorship and pagination error from citation-database metadata; corrected here). The Vindolanda textile corpus — over 700 wool fragments preserved in waterlogged deposits — reveals the material culture of Roman frontier logistics: nearly two-thirds are 2/2 diamond-twill construction, and documented textile supply was dominated by undyed wools supplemented by regional imports (the *tosseae* rugs supplied by trader Gavvo around AD 100, per Vindolanda tablet 192). Wild himself notes that dyestuff analysis shows "red, purple and blue were among the colours favoured by the garrison" (citing Taylor 1983) — he does NOT claim purple was rare; the rarity inference comes from Taylor 1983's 9-of-89 dye-positive rate and the single tentative lichen identification. Wild supplies the scale, waterlogged-preservation context, documentary integration, and textile-vocabulary framework for the Vindolanda corpus. Open (Academia.edu/10508372; Cloudflare may require browser).

<https://www.academia.edu/10508372>

#### De Moor, A., Vanden Berghe, I., Van Strydonck, M., Boudin, M., Fluck, C. (2010) — Radiocarbon dating and dye analysis of Roman linen tunics and dalmatics with purple-coloured design

*Archaeological Textiles Newsletter* 51: 34–47. Radiocarbon dating (IQR 293–411 cal AD; 90% probability 202–512 AD) and HPLC-DAD dye analysis of 21 Roman-period Egyptian linen garments (5 tunics, 9 dalmatics, 7 uncertain) from nine European museum collections, with 19 purple wool decoration threads sampled from 17 garments. The dominant finding: **18 of 19 samples were "false purple" — madder-family anthraquinones (alizarin, purpurin) combined with indigotin/indirubin from indigo or woad**, produced by a two-step mordant+vat dyeing process. Only **one garment** (Brussels ACO.Tx.2477, whose structural analysis is the subject of the companion article by Verhecken-Lammens in the same ATN 51 issue, pp. 31–33) yielded shellfish purple with a composition indicative of *Hexaplex trunculus* (brominated indigoids, no madder; the paper declines a definite species assignment). **Orchil is absent** from all reported results — no phenoxazone-class orcein markers appear in any sample. The article does not explicitly test for orchil as a negative result, and HPLC-DAD is less sensitive for orcein than HPLC-ESI-MS/MS — so the absence is meaningful but not conclusive. Contextualises the madder+indigo imitation tradition (Pliny's "false purples") and suggests orchil's use on Roman-period material was concentrated in specific contexts (parchment codices; northern frontier) rather than being widespread in Egyptian textile production. Open (ATN 51 full issue, atnfriends.com).

<https://www.researchgate.net/publication/279923666>

### Late-antique / Byzantine purple codices (orchil on parchment)

This is the centrepiece of the lichen-in-the-Roman-world story. The three studies below overturn the long-standing assumption that "purple codices" were necessarily dyed with murex Tyrian purple.

#### Aceto, M., et al. (2019) — Mythic dyes or mythic colour? New insight into the use of purple dyes on codices

*Spectrochimica Acta A* 215:133–141. Examined 22 purple codices spanning the 5th–16th centuries (late-antique through Renaissance, with the bulk late-antique and Byzantine) using non-invasive analytical techniques (FORS, spectrofluorimetry, XRF, optical microscopy) plus SERS on micro-samples detached from the Codex Brixianus and Vienna Genesis (the paper's strongest single orchil-identification step). The major finding: Tyrian purple is largely absent from these manuscripts; the majority relied primarily on orchil and folium — cheaper, more readily available alternatives. This overturns the "mythic" association between prestigious purple codices and genuine murex-derived purple. (The bromine-based XRF discrimination used here should be read in light of Aceto et al. 2015, which documents detectable bromine at ~100–200 mg/kg in orchil raw material: bromine *absence* still excludes murex, but low levels do not automatically confirm orchil.) Patronage of the analyzed codices varies per-manuscript and should not be generalized as "imperial." Paywalled (Elsevier).

<https://doi.org/10.1016/j.saa.2019.01.091>

#### Aceto, M., Melo, M. J., Calà, E., Nabais, P., Araújo, R. (2020) — Identification of the purple dye on the Vienna Genesis

Chapter in Hofmann, C. (ed.), *The Vienna Genesis: Material Analysis and Conservation of a Late Antique Illuminated Manuscript on Purple Parchment* (Böhlau Verlag, pp. 103–118; CC BY 4.0 via OAPEN). The Vienna Genesis (Cod. theol. gr. 31, 6th-c. illuminated Greek biblical manuscript, Austrian National Library) was analyzed by FORS (orchil absorption maxima at ~546 and ~588 nm in Log(1/R) coordinates; ~542 and ~586 nm in Kubelka-Munk/F(R) — both sets appear in the chapter), spectrofluorimetry (emission ~623 nm), micro-spectrofluorimetry on aged reference samples, SERS, and handheld XRF for bromine screening. The purple dye was identified as orchil — described in the chapter as "indisputable" — and shellfish purple was excluded (XRF bromine levels suggestive but must be regarded with extreme care as evidence against murex dyeing — Aceto 2020 chapter cites washing, ageing, Br/Hg peak proximity, and absence of absolute reference values as confounders; on the broader interpretive limits of XRF bromine as a murex-vs-orchil discriminator see Aceto et al. 2015). The chapter characterizes the manuscript itself as "probably custom-made for a private owner rather than for liturgical use" — so this specific object is not straightforwardly an "imperial" artifact. Micro-spectrofluorimetry gave the closest match to an aged reference dyed with ***Lasallia pustulata***; *Roccella tinctoria* was not excluded, and SERS alone cannot distinguish between them. The companion parchment chapter (Vnouček et al., pp. 35–70) used eZooMS combined with visual analysis of hair-follicle patterns to identify all 24 folios as sheepskin (raw eZooMS data were mixed due to 1975 conservation-glue contamination). A separate chapter (Hofmann et al., pp. 201–245) used µ-XRF, FORS, Raman, and SERS on the miniatures themselves to identify seven distinct painters by pigment palette. The companion Cod. ser. n. 2804 fragment gave identical dye results (orchil). Open access (CC BY 4.0 via OAPEN / Internet Archive).

<https://library.oapen.org/handle/20.500.12657/41206>

#### Bicchieri, M., Monti, M., Piantanida, G., Sodo, A. (2014) — The Purple Codex Rossanensis: spectroscopic characterisation and first evidence of elderberry lake in a sixth-century manuscript

*Environmental Science and Pollution Research* 21(24):14146–14157. Spectroscopic characterization (FORS, micro-Raman, micro-FTIR, XRF — FORS with Gaussian deconvolution was the decisive technique for dye identification) of the Codex Purpureus Rossanensis (6th-century Byzantine illuminated Gospel, Museo Diocesano Rossano Calabria, Italy). The purple parchment was identified as orchil (from *Roccella tinctoria*) prepared with sodium carbonate, following Stockholm Papyrus recipes 131 and 123; XRF showed no bromine, ruling out Tyrian purple. The paper's headline finding is the **first reported identification of elderberry (*Sambucus nigra*) lake** — a cyanidin-based anthocyanin pigment — as the red/violet organic lake used throughout the miniatures. An identical micro-FTIR purple-parchment spectrum from the companion Codex Purpureus Sarezzanensis (5th–6th c.) suggests that codex also was not dyed with Tyrian purple as principal dye. XRF found no bromine even after one-hour acquisitions, and the bromine-based argument should be read alongside Aceto et al. 2015's finding of ~100–200 mg/kg bromine in orchil raw material — murex-level bromine signals are absent. Bicchieri 2014 *predates* the Aceto series on purple codices by five years and is the earliest published report consistent with orchil (not shellfish) on a 6th-c. Byzantine illuminated manuscript; note that the paper's own "first evidence" language is restricted to the elderberry lake identification, and the orchil identification itself is hedged by the authors. Open (arXiv preprint 1404.6414).

<https://doi.org/10.1007/s11356-014-3341-6>

## Reference Volumes

Standard monographs. Each is essential; check library and ILL before purchasing.

#### Cardon, D. (2007) — *Natural Dyes: Sources, Tradition, Technology and Science*

Archetype Publications, London. 778 pp. ISBN 978-1904982005. Encyclopedic monograph covering approximately 300 colorant sources across all continents, combining botanical, zoological, and chemical detail with historical recipes. Part 2 ("Dyes from lichens and fungi") covers orchil production from *Roccella tinctoria* and related species with their chemical processing; Part 3 covers Mediterranean shellfish purple. The standard encyclopedic reference for natural dye studies; cited in virtually every subsequent analytical investigation of shellfish and lichen purples. Book.

<https://archetype.co.uk/our-titles/natural-dyes/>

#### Hofenk de Graaff, J. H., Roelofs, W. G. Th., van Bommel, M. R. (2004) — *The Colourful Past: Origins, Chemistry and Identification of Natural Dyestuffs*

Archetype / Abegg-Stiftung. Standard scholarly reference for textile conservators and dye chemists, covering the origins, chemistry, and analytical identification of the major classes of historical natural dyestuffs. Each chapter pairs historical and botanical context with detailed HPLC-DAD reference data (retention times, UV-Vis spectra, characteristic marker compounds) for lichen-derived orchil/orcein alongside anthraquinones, indigoids, and flavonoids. Together with Cardon (2007), the two foundational monographs in the field. Book.

#### Huneck, S., Yoshimura, I. (1996) — *Identification of Lichen Substances*

Springer. Catalogs approximately 700 lichen secondary metabolites with structures, spectral data (UV, IR, MS, NMR), and chromatographic identification methods (TLC, HPLC). Covers lecanoric acid, erythrin, and gyrophoric acid — the key orcinol-type depsides that serve as precursors to orcein in the orchil dye process — in the book's standard reference-handbook format (structure, MW, melting point, microcrystallization plates, and spectral data entries). Standard chemical reference for cataloguing lichen secondary metabolites compound-by-compound, with species cross-references; encompasses the depside and depsidone classes to which orchil precursors belong. Book.

## Lichen Taxonomy and Biology

These papers establish the correct names and phylogenetic distribution of the orchil-producing species. Note that *Roccella fucoides* as used in all pre-2003 dye literature is now *R. phycopsis* per Tehler 2003.

#### Frezza, C., et al. (2024) — Evaluation of the antifungal and antiproliferative properties of the lichen *Roccella tinctoria* DC.

*Pharmaceutics* 16(3):331. One of the first systematic LC-MS phytochemical profiles of *Roccella tinctoria* collected from the central Mediterranean (Ostia Antica, near Rome) using non-aqueous extraction (SPME-GC-MS and column chromatography; the paper's novelty claim is scoped to non-aqueous extracts of Italian material — prior aqueous-extract studies exist), identifying 20 volatile and 3 non-volatile constituents: erythrin, methyl orsellinate, and montagnetol. Confirms that the depside chemistry of *R. tinctoria* growing along the Italian coastline — the very lichen accessible to Roman-era producers — is anchored in erythrin and lecanoric-acid-class depsides, providing modern analytical confirmation of the precursor chemistry relevant to Roman-era dye production. Open access (MDPI).

<https://doi.org/10.3390/pharmaceutics16030331>

#### Tehler, A. (2003) — *Roccella phycopsis* the correct name for *R. fucoides*

*Taxon* 52(3):621. Short nomenclatural note establishing that *Roccella phycopsis* Ach. (Acharius 1810) is the priority name for the lichen previously and widely known as *R. fucoides* (Neck.) Vain. **Practical consequence for all dye scholarship:** all pre-2003 literature describing *R. fucoides* as a Mediterranean orchil-producing lichen refers to the species now correctly called *R. phycopsis*. Paywalled (JSTOR).

<https://doi.org/10.2307/3647467>

#### Tehler, A., Irestedt, M. (2007) — Parallel evolution of lichen growth forms in the family Roccellaceae

*Cladistics* 23(5):432–454. Multi-locus molecular phylogeny of the Roccellaceae demonstrating that the fruticose (shrubby) growth forms characteristic of orchil-bearing *Roccella* species arose independently multiple times within the family rather than from a single ancestor — a striking case of convergent morphological evolution. Provides the phylogenetic backbone for understanding the generic limits and growth-form diversity of orchil-bearing Roccellaceae; species-accessibility inferences for Roman-period collectors rely on the biogeographic partition established in Tehler et al. 2009 (below), not on this paper. Paywalled (Wiley).

<https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1096-0031.2007.00156.x>

#### Tehler, A., Irestedt, M., Wedin, M., Ertz, D. (2009) — Origin, evolution and taxonomy of American *Roccella*

*Systematics and Biodiversity* 7(3):307–317. Molecular phylogeny showing that American *Roccella* species form a monophyletic group most closely related to Macaronesian species rather than to the principal Old World Mediterranean clade; the paper's historical framework is plate-tectonic vicariance (late Jurassic to late Cretaceous), with no discussion of Roman, medieval, or dye-trade history. **Biogeographic implication (survey synthesis, not a claim of Tehler 2009):** since the American and Canary Islands *Roccella* species form a disjunct monophyletic group distinct from the Mediterranean clade (Tehler et al. 2009), the post-c. 1500 CE American and Canary Islands orchilla trade supplying European dyers (Kok 1966; Smith 1921) drew on populations unavailable to Roman-era Mediterranean collectors; Roman orchil would have been confined to Mediterranean littoral populations of *R. tinctoria* and close relatives. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1017/S1477200009990065>

#### Tehler, A., Irestedt, M., Wedin, M., Ertz, D. (2010) — The Old World *Roccella* species outside Europe and Macaronesia

*Systematics and Biodiversity* 8(2):223–246. Revises Old World *Roccella* from Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and South and East Asia using four molecular markers, delimiting eight species and describing two new ones. Establishes which orchil-bearing *Roccella* taxa were available in Old World regions that would have served as sources or trade conduits for Roman-era purple and red-purple dyes. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1080/14772001003789554>

#### Ertz, D., Tehler, A., Irestedt, M., Frisch, A., Thor, G., van den Boom, P. (2014) — A large-scale phylogenetic revision of Roccellaceae

*Fungal Diversity* 70:31–53. Large-scale two-locus phylogenetic revision of the family Roccellaceae (~341 sequences, ~180 specimens, ~114 species), finding four established genera to be paraphyletic or polyphyletic and describing eight new genera. Any use of "Roccella" or related genera as dye sources in historical or archaeochemical literature must be evaluated against this revised generic framework. Paywalled (Springer).

<https://doi.org/10.1007/s13225-014-0286-5>

## Ethnobotanical and Practitioner Literature

**Flagged** where practitioner rather than peer-reviewed. Include for process reconstruction and comparative context; cite the analytical literature for chemical claims.

#### Grierson, S. (1986) — *The Colour Cauldron*

Mill Books, Perth. Definitive English-language survey of Scottish natural-dye folk practice, covering plant, mineral, and lichen sources from antiquity through the post-medieval period. Gives particular weight to orchil-type lichen traditions of the Scottish Highlands and Hebrides, documenting the use of *Ochrolechia tartarea* (korkir/corkir) as an ammonia-fermented purple-red dye, and situates these surviving folk traditions within a broader European orchil lineage. Best single reference for northern-European lichen purple dye practice outside the Mediterranean. Co-founding document of the Dyes in History and Archaeology (DHA) symposium series. Book.

#### Grierson, S., Duff, D. G., Sinclair, R. S. (1985a) — The Colour and Fastness of Natural Dyes of the Scottish Highlands

*J. Society of Dyers and Colourists* 101(7–8):220–228. Quantitatively characterizes the colour and fastness of wool dyed with Scottish Highland plant and lichen colorants using CIELAB and Munsell notation. Key finding for Roman dye studies: lichen-derived purples (including orchil-type dyes) exhibit poor lightfastness (typically ISO Blue Wool grade 1–2), explaining why orchil purples may have faded below visual and analytical detection limits on excavated Roman textiles even when originally present in significant quantities. Paywalled (Wiley).

<https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1478-4408.1985.tb01026.x>

#### Grierson, S., Duff, D. G., Sinclair, R. S. (1985b) — Natural Dyes of the Scottish Highlands

*Textile History* 16(1):23–43. Ethnobotanical and historical survey drawing on Gaelic oral tradition, estate records, and travellers' accounts to document lichen dye species, mordanting practice, and colour ranges in Scotland. Provides an ethnohistorical analogue for reconstructing pre-industrial lichen dye workflows and demonstrates the continuity of fermentation-based purple dyeing as living craft knowledge beyond the classical period. Paywalled (T&F).

<https://doi.org/10.1179/004049685793701223>

#### Brough, S. G. (1988) — Navajo Lichen Dyes

*The Lichenologist* 20(3):279–290. Conducted 155 dye tests on 35 lichen species from the Navajo Reservation using boiling-water extraction, ammonia fermentation, and DMSO solvent extraction, correlating resulting hues with lichen secondary metabolite profiles. Methodological analogue demonstrating how extraction method shifts orcein/chromophore ratios and thus hue; useful for understanding process variability in ancient orchil production across culturally distinct contexts. Paywalled (CUP).

<https://doi.org/10.1017/S0024282988000313>

#### Casselman, K. D. (2001) — *Lichen Dyes: The New Source Book* (2nd rev. ed.)

Dover. ISBN 9780486412313. **Practitioner handbook; not peer-reviewed.** Covers dye-lichen identification, ammonia fermentation and boiling-water extraction chemistry, ecological cautions about sustainable harvesting, and an extensive historical bibliography tracing lichen dyeing from antiquity through the industrial era. Species-by-colour charts and documentation of regional traditions make it a useful cross-reference alongside analytical findings on orchil and orcein-class compounds. Book.

#### Bolton, E. M. (1960) — *Lichens for Vegetable Dyeing*

Studio Vista. **Practitioner handbook; not peer-reviewed; taxonomy and chemistry now superseded.** Mid-20th-century craft-dyer reference with hand-dyed wool swatches; chemistry is entirely pre-Musso (1960) and describes dye behaviour empirically without reference to the orcinol-oxidation pathway or orcein chromophore structures. Serves today as a historical-ethnographic document of the mid-20th-century craft-revival movement. Book.

#### Smith, A. L. (1921) — *Lichens*

Cambridge Botanical Handbooks. Public domain; Project Gutenberg 61616; Internet Archive ia:cu31924001614480. Chapter X ("Economic and Technical") traces orchil-producing lichens from classical antiquity through the industrial era: Pliny's *Phycos Thalassion* is identified with *Roccella* ("crisp leaves, used in Crete for dyeing garments"), and Theophrastus's Cretan plants used to dye wool are similarly linked to the Roccellaceae. Smith *asserts* the *Roccella* identification without detailed philological engagement; the fuller argument is in Kok 1966 and Whitworth & Koren 2016. Smith also opens her dyeing section with Ezekiel 27:7 as evidence that lichen dyeing "dates back to a remote antiquity" — Whitworth & Koren 2016 (p. 249 n. 7) reject the Ezekiel identification on Hebrew-lexical grounds. Smith further reports that "the main supply of 'weeds' came from the Levant until the fifteenth century when supplies were obtained from the Canaries" (p. 412) — geographically consistent with Roman-accessible eastern-Mediterranean *Roccella* populations. Recounts the 14th-century Florentine rediscovery of *Roccella* by Federigo, the ammonia-fermentation chemistry, and preparation routes for orchil, cudbear, litmus, and "French purple." Readers should note Smith uses pre-molecular species names: *R. fucoides* (= *R. phycopsis* per Tehler 2003), *Lecanora tartarea* (= *Ochrolechia tartarea*), *Umbilicaria pustulata* (= *Lasallia pustulata*). Use for historical background; taxonomy pre-dates molecular systematics. Open access (Internet Archive).

<https://archive.org/details/cu31924001614480>

## Primary Ancient Texts

Entries focused on lichen-relevant passages, grouped by tradition. No ancient author uses the word "orchil" or "orseille" — those are medieval terms first attested in the 14th century (Kok 1966). Ancient authors use Greek *phykos* / Latin *fucus* (both ambiguous between alga, lichen, and cosmetic pigment) and the narrower *phykos thalassion* ("sea-phycos"). The identification of any of these passages with *Roccella*-type lichen is a modern philological inference (Kok 1966, Smith 1921), not an ancient claim. Passages are flagged accordingly. See the Stage 8 primary-source report at `.work/stage8/primary-sources-report.md` for full passage texts, edition details, and verification notes.

### Greek and Latin textile / dye passages

#### Theophrastus — *Historia Plantarum* (c. 350–287 BCE), HP 4.6.5; trans. A. Hort, Loeb Classical Library 70 (1916)

HP 4.6.5 (in Hort LCL 70, vol. I, Books I–V) describes a coastal plant growing abundantly on the rocks of Crete, used for dyeing ribbons, wool, and clothing, whose colour is praised as more beautiful than the purple dye; it occurs most abundantly and most finely on the north coast of the island. **HP 4.6.5 contains no three-type typology and no leaf-morphology description for the Cretan dyeing plant.** The tripartite classification of *phykos thalassion* (broad-leafed / reddish / curly-leafed Cretan) belongs to Pliny NH 13.135–136 (L370) and Dioscorides DM IV.98, not to Theophrastus; the curly-leaf detail (*phyllois oulon*) is Dioscorides' specifically (see Dioscorides entry below). The location-and-use description — rocky coast, north-facing exposure, dyeing of wool and cloth — is the primary basis for Kok's (1966) and Smith's (1921) identification of the Cretan dyeing plant with *Roccella tinctoria*; the identification is contested and the term *phykos* was not taxonomically precise in Greek usage. #### Pliny the Elder — *Naturalis Historia* (c. 77–79 CE); trans. H. Rackham / W. H. S. Jones, Loeb Classical Library

**Umbrella entry covering four volumes, each locally held.** The orchil-relevant passages span three books:

- **NH 13.135–136** (Loeb L370; `research_documents/Pliny_NH_Loeb_L370_books_12-16.pdf`). Chapter XLVIII "Phycos, Prason, or Zoster." Pliny states explicitly that "*phycos* is a shrub" (*frutex*) whereas "our word *alga* denotes a herbaceous sea-plant" — the terminological distinction between shrub and herb is the cornerstone of the lichen identification argument (Kok 1966 p. 249; Smith 1921 p. 191). He then adds: "The *phycos* growing on rocks round the island of Crete is also used for a purple dye; the most approved kind being that growing on the northern side of the island." Rock-growing habit and a north-facing exposure preference are consistent with known *Roccella tinctoria* ecology. Identification CONTESTED; this is the primary terminological anchor for the lichen reading (the *frutex* vs. *alga* distinction). Open via Wikisource and Attalus (see URLs below).
- **NH 26.103** (Loeb L393; `research_documents/Pliny_NH_Loeb_L393_books_24-27.pdf`; NH 26.66 in the Loeb chapter numbering corresponds to §103 in the continuous Latin section numbering; both conventions appear in this bibliography). In a medical context, Pliny distinguishes three types of *phycos thalassion* matching Dioscorides DM IV.98 almost exactly: (1) broad-leafed, (2) reddish, (3) curly-leafed Cretan type — described as "used as a ground-colour for the purple of the murex." This "ground-colour" function (*fucus marinus qui sub murice sternitur*) is the most important dye-chemistry statement in Pliny for orchil interpretation: it positions *phycos* as a base dye applied beneath Tyrian, exactly the role Kok (1966) reconstructs for orchil. Identification CONTESTED but this is the passage with the highest confidence for the lichen reading.
- **NH 22.3** (Loeb L392; `research_documents/Pliny_NH_Loeb_L392_books_20-23.pdf`). Notes that "Transalpine Gaul can produce with vegetable dyes Tyrian purple, oyster purple, and all other colours." No specific plant is named; *phycos* and *fucus* do not appear. This passage gives context for plant-based purple substitution across the Roman world but does NOT identify orchil or lichen. **Bibliographic correction:** an earlier version of this entry cited "Book 22.3 (fucus marinus)" — the Stage 8 report confirms this is a conflation; the *fucus marinus* / *phykos thalassion* passages are in Books 13 and 26, not Book 22.
- **NH 35.44–46** (Loeb L394; `research_documents/Pliny_NH_Loeb_L394_books_33-35.pdf`). Treats *purpurissum* — a painter's pigment lake made by steeping *creta argentaria* (silversmiths' earth) in a murex-dye vat — and notes that Pozzuoli purpurissum "combines most easily with *hysginum* and madder." This is a pigment, not a textile dye, and neither *hysginum* nor *purpurissum* is identified with orchil in any modern scholarly consensus. Context for the multi-colorant approach to purple painting; NOT a lichen passage. **Cross-reference:** *Purpurissum* is exclusively Pliny's term in this corpus; the Vitruvius 7.13 entry has been corrected to remove an erroneous attribution of *purpurissum* to Vitruvius (see Vitruvius entry above). Also: **NH 9.102–140** (Loeb L353; `research_documents/Pliny_NH_Loeb_L353_books_8-11.pdf`) covers murex purple in detail — geographical quality rankings, price history, adulterating combinations — and is the essential backdrop for understanding what orchil competed with and imitated.

#### Dioscorides, Pedanius — *De Materia Medica* (1st c. CE), DM IV.98; trans. T. A. Osbaldeston (2000) / L. Y. Beck (2005)

Key passage: **DM IV.98** in Wellmann's Greek edition / Beck's English translation (alternately IV.99 or IV.100 in other editions; Osbaldeston uses IV.100). Dioscorides distinguishes three varieties of *phykos thalassion* (this three-type typology and the curly-leaf morphology below belong to Dioscorides and Pliny, not to Theophrastus HP 4.6.5): (1) broad-leafed, (2) inclining to red, (3) the curly-leafed Cretan type (*phyllois oulon*) used to dye garments — noting also its use as a ground colour preparatory to shellfish-purple dyeing and its medicinal applications (joint diseases, gout). The three-part typology corresponds so closely to Pliny NH 26.103 that both authors evidently drew on a shared pharmacognostic tradition. The curly-leafed Cretan type is the most plausible *Roccella* candidate in both authors; identification CONTESTED (Kok 1966 p. 249–252). Beck's translation (Olms-Weidmann, *Altertumswissenschaftliche Texte und Studien* 38) is the only complete modern English rendering, based on Wellmann's authoritative Greek edition (1906–1914); the Osbaldeston translation is open-access. **Corpus note:** the locally-held Osbaldeston translation at IV.100 describes the third variety as "white, growing in Crete near the ground" and does **not** contain the curly-leafed (*phyllois oulon*), garment-dyeing, or ground-colour-for-murex details. Those dyeing specifics are present in the Wellmann Greek edition and the Beck English translation at IV.98 — they are independently corroborated by Pliny NH 26.103 in this corpus — but they are not in Osbaldeston. Cite Beck (2005) or Wellmann (1906–1914) for the dyeing passage, not Osbaldeston (2000). Locally held: `research_documents/Dioscorides_DeMateriaMedica_Osbaldeston_2000.pdf`. ### Greek craft / alchemical recipe papyri

#### Stockholm Papyrus (*Papyrus Graecus Holmiensis*, 3rd–4th c. CE) — Recipes S108–S110; ed. Halleux 1981 (Budé); Jensen 2008 (UC OA)

**THE ancient orchil recipe source, and one of the most directly usable ancient recipe reconstructions — specifically, the only ancient orchil recipe experimentally reconstructed in modern work (Whitworth et al. 2025) — establishing that lichen purple was an explicit commercial product in the Roman period.** Recipe **S109** ("Dyeing in Phoenician Colour with Archil"): wool is pre-treated with ash, mordanted with alum in salt water, then cooked with three times the weight of *archil* (orchil) in rainwater; goat's milk is poured in and stirred; wool is immersed until saturated, then rinsed and dried away from smoke. This is the recipe experimentally reconstructed in Whitworth, Chen & Smith (2025). The dye is explicitly called "Phoenician colour" — equating lichen purple with the prestige register of Tyrian shellfish purple. Recipe **S110** ("Dyeing in Bright Red Purple"): grind archil, take 5 cyathi of the juice per mina of wool; for a brighter tint add natron; for still brighter, chalcanthum (copper sulphate). **The papyrus uses the Greek word *archil* (ἀρχίλ)** — a commercial trade term unambiguously denoting lichen orchil, distinct from the classical *phykos* — making this the earliest surviving datable use of an "orchil"-type word and the ONLY ancient source to use a term unambiguously referring to lichen orchil. No seaweed alternative produces orcein chromophores; the orchil identification is chemically confirmed by Whitworth et al. (2025). Critical edition: Halleux, R. *Les Alchimistes grecs*, Tome I: *Papyrus de Leyde, Papyrus de Stockholm, Fragments de recettes*. Paris: Les Belles Lettres (Budé), 1981 (OCLC 7742382). Partial English translation: Caley, E. R. *Journal of Chemical Education* 4(8):979 (1927) [also cited as Caley 1932 in some sources, including Jensen 2008, which gives 1932 as the date of the revised edition; both dates appear in this bibliography]. Locally held: `research_documents/Jensen_2008_Leyden_Stockholm_papyri.pdf` (Jensen 2008, UC OA).

<https://homepages.uc.edu/~jensenwb/books/Leyden%20&%20Stockholm%20Papyri.pdf>

#### Leyden Papyrus X (*Papyrus Graecus Leidensis X*, 3rd–4th c. CE) — Recipe §95; co-ed. Halleux 1981; Jensen 2008

Co-edited with the Stockholm Papyrus in Halleux 1981 (same Budé volume); partial English translation: Caley, E. R. *Journal of Chemical Education* 3(10):1149 (1926). Contains 111 recipes for imitation gold, silver, precious stones, and purple dyes. **Recipe §95** ("The Preparation of Purple"): "Break into small pieces stone of Phrygia [a mordant]; put it to boiling, and having immersed the wool, leave it until it cools. Then throwing in the vessel a mina of *seaweed* [Greek: *phykos*], put it to boiling and throw in again a mina of *seaweed*... throw in the water an imperceptible quantity of copperas, in order to develop the purple." **Identification AMBIGUOUS.** Unlike the Stockholm Papyrus, the Leyden Papyrus does not use the word *archil*; the *phykos* of §95 could be a red alga (*Porphyra* or similar Rhodophyta, which yield reddish-purple tints) or a lichen. The copperas (iron sulphate) colour-developer step is consistent with a polyphenol-based dye substrate but is not characteristic of orchil/orcein chemistry; Halleux (1981) flags the ambiguity. **Do not assert "orchil" for this recipe without qualification.** Adjacent recipes (§96 onward) use alkanet, walnuts, and gall-nuts for purple imitation — plant dyes, not lichen. Locally held: `research_documents/Jensen_2008_Leyden_Stockholm_papyri.pdf`; Wikisource transcription open.

<https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Leyden_Papyrus_X>

### Late-antique and early-medieval sources

#### Cassiodorus — *Variae* (c. 537 CE), Book I Letter 2; ed. Mommsen (MGH 1894); trans. Hodgkin 1886 / Bjornlie 2019

*Variae* I.2 is a directive from Cassiodorus (writing in the voice of King Theodoric) to one Theon concerning a delayed shipment of royal garments dyed with imperial *purpura* from workshops at Hydruntum (Otranto). The letter elaborates the natural history of murex and the prestige of shellfish-derived purple. **NEGATIVE FINDING: the purple described is murex-only; no orchil, *phycos*, or lichen reference appears.** Relevant as late-antique confirmation that the Gothic court at Ravenna maintained the Roman imperial tradition of shellfish-purple vestments — the prestige backdrop against which orchil's use as a substitute (documented in the Vienna Genesis, Codex Rossanensis) should be understood. Critical edition: Mommsen, T. (ed.). MGH, Auctores Antiquissimi 12. Berlin, 1894. Complete English translation: Bjornlie, M. S. *Cassiodorus: The Variae*. Oakland: UC Press, 2019 (paywalled). Partial translation (locally held): `research_documents/Cassiodorus_Variae_Hodgkin_1886_IA.pdf` (Hodgkin 1886).

<https://www.gutenberg.org/files/18590/18590-h/18590-h.htm>

#### Isidore of Seville — *Etymologiae* (c. 615–636 CE), Book 19.28 (*De coloribus vestium*); ed. Lindsay (OCT 1911); trans. Barney et al. (2006)

*Etymologiae* 19.28 "On the Colours of Garments" defines *tinctura* (dye), *kokkos* / *vermiculus* (kermes), *conchylium* / *ostrum* (shellfish purple from sea-shells), and *purpura* (purple named "from the purity of light"). **NEGATIVE FINDING: no mention of *fucus*, orchil, lichen, *phycos*, or plant-derived purple substitutes appears in 19.28.** Isidore's garment-colour chapter is entirely murex- and kermes-centred. Book 17 (*De Agricultura*) may contain plant-dye terminology but no orchil passage has been identified in secondary literature. Useful for closing the search space: the *phycos/fucus* tradition documented in Pliny and Dioscorides did not survive into Isidore's early-medieval encyclopaedia. Latin text: Lindsay, W. M. (ed.). OCT, 1911; open via LacusCurtius (link below). English: Barney, S. A. et al. Cambridge UP, 2006 (ISBN 9780521145916; paywalled).

A miscellany of practical craft recipes on pigments, metals, dyes, and related arts; the earliest complete manuscript dates to the 9th century, but the recipes incorporate older Roman-era traditions, functioning as a bridge document between the papyrus recipe-book tradition and the medieval West. #### Pseudo-Aristotle — *De Coloribus* (c. 3rd c. BCE, Peripatetic school); trans. W. S. Hett, Loeb Classical Library 307 (1936)

A systematic treatise on the physics of colour perception and colour mixing. Section 4 introduces "purple [from] the murex" among colouring agents; the process description of shellfish-purple preparation (boiling stages, colour changes) appears in Sections 4–5 as an analogy for fruit ripening. **NEGATIVE FINDING: there is no mention of lichen, *phycos*, *fucus*, or any plant-derived purple substitute in *De Coloribus*.** The work is a theoretical colour treatise, not a practical dye manual; its purple account is shellfish-centred throughout. ## Historical and Economic Context

Brief entries. These illuminate why orchil proliferated and what its social position was relative to murex purple.

#### Wouters, J., Vanden Berghe, I., Richard, G., Breniaux, R., Cardon, D. (2008) — Dye analysis of selected textiles from three Roman sites in the Eastern Desert of Egypt

*Dyes in History and Archaeology* 21:1–16. HPLC-PDA (photodiode-array; functionally equivalent to HPLC-DAD) analysis of 94 dyes from 74 textiles at praesidia Maximianon, Krokodilô (Koptos–Myos Hormos road) and Didymoi (Koptos–Berenike road), dated 1st–3rd century AD (with the Roman-period cohort sensu stricto 1st–2nd c. and 3rd-c. material classified as transitional to Coptic). The headline finding is direct chemical evidence of faked Tyrian purple: 92% of Roman-Egyptian purple samples combine madder-type anthraquinones with an indigoid component to imitate true murex purple (*purpura alia* / *purpura hysginum*), while true shellfish purple appears in only four Roman-period samples (one pure, three in combination with kermes and/or madder). The paper explicitly states (p. 12) that **"lichens and alkanet dyes were not found in Roman or Coptic Egyptian textiles"** — an affirmative negative result for orchil in the Eastern Desert corpus, not merely an absence from the reported hits. This paper establishes the substitution economy within which orchil operated: demand for "purple" at all price points was met by multiple chemically distinct surrogates, with madder+indigo dominant in Egypt and orchil essentially absent. Open (HAL preprint; browser required due to Anubis challenge).

<https://shs.hal.science/halshs-00369852>

#### Graves, D. E. (2017) — What is the Madder with Lydia's Purple? A Reexamination of the Purpurarii in Thyatira and Philippi

*Near East Archaeological Society Bulletin* 62:3–28. Reexamines epigraphic and archaeological evidence for *purpurarii* (purple dye dealers) at Thyatira and Philippi, arguing that the guild handled a quality spectrum from genuine shellfish dye to plant-based substitutes. ## Summary of Key Findings

### Orchil chemistry

Orchil is produced from lichen depsides (chiefly erythrin, lecanoric acid, and gyrophoric acid) by ammonia fermentation: the lichen thallus is steeped in ammonia solution with aeration, converting colourless orcinol (3,5-dihydroxytoluene) to purple orcein chromophores. Musso (1960) and Beecken et al. (1961/2003) established the structural basis: orcein is a mixture of 14 characterised phenoxazone-based components (8 major + 6 minor per Beecken et al. 1961 Table 1) divided into the 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone series (α/β/γ-hydroxyorcein) and the 7-amino-2-phenoxazone series (α/β/γ-aminoorcein). Litmus is a polymeric assembly of the hydroxy series (Beecken et al. 1961/2003). The dye is pH-dependent (red in acid, deep-violet in alkali per Beecken et al. 1961/2003 Table 1; Clementi 2006 reports an aqueous solution λmax shift from 510 nm acidic to 570 nm basic, i.e. purple to blue-purple) and strongly photolabile, particularly the β- and γ-orcein fractions that are primarily responsible for purple hues (Roca et al. 2026 preprint). Modern HPLC-ESI-MS/MS targeting these six isomers via SRM (selected reaction monitoring; the community-equivalent term is MRM) is now the sensitive method of choice, established by Witkowski et al. (2017) as the first LC-MS/MS method covering all major orcein components.

Species-specific fingerprinting is now possible: Calà et al. (2019) identified 13 characteristic precursor ions and 32 compounds distinguishing *Lasallia pustulata*, *Ochrolechia tartarea*, and *Roccella tinctoria* by PCA of HPLC-MS/MS profiles. Serafini et al. (2018) demonstrated complementary discrimination via HPTLC, SERS, and HPLC-MS. Non-invasive pre-screening via FORS (orchil reflectance maxima at ~542–546 and ~586–588 nm depending on coordinate system — Kubelka-Munk vs. log(1/R)) and spectrofluorimetry allows tentative identification before sampling; emission maxima shift substantially with substrate and substrate age — Clementi 2006 Table 1 reports orcein solution emission ~595 nm but fresh orcein-dyed silk at ~640 nm and wool at ~650 nm, while aged purple parchment emits at ~623 nm (Aceto 2015, 2020). One consequential side-finding: Aceto et al. (2015) detected significant bromine (up to ~200 mg/kg) in *R. tinctoria* raw material and in finished orchil dye, so XRF-based discrimination of orchil from Tyrian purple is quantitative (concentration regime compatible with murex vs. with orchil + terrestrial background), not presence/absence. The codex-paradox XRF-no-bromine arguments below should be read in that light.

### Lichen sources

The primary Mediterranean orchil source is *Roccella* — chiefly *R. tinctoria* and what pre-2003 literature calls *R. fucoides* (= *R. phycopsis* per Tehler 2003, the priority name; all older dye-history literature must be read accordingly). Frezza et al. (2024) confirmed that *R. tinctoria* growing at Ostia Antica (near Rome) contains erythrin (a lecanoric-acid-class depside glycoside) as the dominant non-volatile constituent of cold-methanol extracts, alongside methyl orsellinate and montagnetol — the precursor chemistry for orchil production was available along the Italian coastline in antiquity. (Note: free lecanoric acid was not detected as such in Frezza et al.; erythrin hydrolyses to lecanoric acid + erythritol during the ammonia-fermentation process that produces orcein. This apparent species-chemistry tension — Zopf 1907 / Smith 1921 had assigned *lecanoric acid* to *R. tinctoria* and erythrin to *R. fuciformis* / *R. fucoides* — likely reflects early-20th-c. extraction workflows hydrolyzing erythrin to free lecanoric acid during analysis rather than a genuine chemosyndromic difference.) The Tehler phylogenetic series (2007, 2009, 2010) establishes that the American and Canary Islands *Roccella* trade that supplied 16th–20th century Europe was inaccessible to Roman producers; Roman orchil was confined to Mediterranean littoral populations (Smith 1921 p. 412 confirms a pre-15th-c. Levantine supply chain, geographically consistent with the Roman-accessible eastern Mediterranean). Northern European and Scottish practice used *Ochrolechia tartarea* (yielding cudbear and korkir) and *Lasallia pustulata* (Grierson 1986; Grierson et al. 1985b); these are the species found in medieval English and Central European artifacts by Calà et al. (2019).

The Pliny/Dioscorides *phycos thalassion* identification remains debated and is load-bearing for the survey's claim of lichen purple in Greco-Roman antiquity. Kok (1966) and Smith (1921) both endorse the *Roccella* reading of Pliny NH XIII.136 and Theophrastus — neither presents a detailed philological argument; Kok inherits the identification from Forbes (1956) and Smith herself. Whitworth & Koren (2016) supply the fullest modern philological discussion (noting that the Index of Plants in the Loeb Theophrastus translation (Hort) attests six different plant-referents for Greek *phycos*). The underlying Pliny passage (NH XIII.136) is in Loeb L370, which is locally held as `research_documents/Pliny_NH_Loeb_L370_books_12-16.pdf` (Rackham translation, Books XII–XVI); NH 13.135–136 has been directly audited. Readers should also consult Kok 1966 and Whitworth & Koren 2016 (p. 248–249 and footnote 7) alongside the Beck Dioscorides translation. The same note (Whitworth & Koren 2016, p. 249 n. 7) **rejects** the Ezekiel 27:7 lichen-dye identification that Smith 1921 and Kok 1966 invoke — *tekhelet* and *argaman* are Hebrew shellfish-dye terms.

### Roman-period textile evidence

Taylor (1983) on pre-Hadrianic Vindolanda is the foundational Roman textile identification, but its scope is narrow: **a single sample (10/53) out of 89 tested was tentatively identified as a lichen purple resembling orchil**, on the basis of UV-visible absorption spectrophotometry (pyridine/water extract, Mg(OAc)₂ shift, pH diagnostics) plus paper chromatography on selected samples. Taylor's method cannot discriminate between *Roccella*, *Ochrolechia*, and *Umbilicaria*/*Lasallia* — the family-level "Roccellaceae" attribution used in earlier scholarship (and earlier drafts of this survey) is stricter than what the paper supports; Taylor's own wording is "a lichen purple dye" / "may be the Roman orchil." The "poor person's purple" framing is later (Kok 1966; Cardon 2007), not Taylor's own. Taylor's 9-of-89 overall dye-positive rate and 1-of-9 tentative-lichen rate imply that purple dyes were rare across the full Vindolanda fragment assemblage — an inference from Taylor 1983, not a claim in Wild (2011); Wild's own gloss is that "red, purple and blue were among the colours favoured by the garrison" (p. 71). Wild (2011) supplies the scale (700+ wool fragments), the waterlogged preservation context, and the textile-vocabulary framework. De Moor et al. (2010) — in ATN 51 pp. 34–47, distinct from the companion Verhecken-Lammens structural piece in the same issue — found **no orchil** in a large HPLC-DAD analyzed corpus of 21 late-Roman Egyptian linen tunics and dalmatics; the purple was overwhelmingly madder+indigo "false purple" (18 of 19 sampled threads), with genuine shellfish purple present in only one garment (Brussels ACO.Tx.2477). Wouters et al. (2008) reach an even stronger negative result on 94 analyses from 74 textiles at Maximianon, Krokodilô, and Didymoi: the paper explicitly concludes (p. 12) that **"lichens and alkanet dyes were not found in Roman or Coptic Egyptian textiles"** — an explicit negative for orchil, not merely an absence from the reported hits. Both 1st–3rd c. Eastern Desert (Wouters 2008; HPLC-PDA) and 3rd–5th c. Nile-valley linens (De Moor 2010; HPLC-DAD) show the same pattern: the dominant non-murex Roman-Egyptian purple was madder+indigo, not orchil. Orchil's footprint on Roman-period textile assemblages is real but sparse — expected given the dye's extreme photolability (Grierson et al. 1985a; Roca et al. 2026) and HPLC-DAD/PDA's lower sensitivity for orcein compared with HPLC-ESI-MS/MS.

### The purple codex paradox

The most consequential finding in recent lichen-dye scholarship is that late-antique "purple codices" — long assumed to represent the apex of murex-dyed luxury — were in most cases dyed with orchil or folium, not shellfish purple. Bicchieri et al. (2014) first identified the Codex Rossanensis parchment as orchil (from *Roccella tinctoria*) prepared with sodium carbonate (following Stockholm Papyrus recipes 131 and 123), with no XRF-detectable bromine, and proposed the same identification tentatively for the companion Codex Purpureus Sarezzanensis on the basis of identical micro-FTIR parchment spectra; Aceto et al. (2019) surveyed 22 purple codices and found Tyrian purple largely absent across the corpus; Aceto et al. (2020) identified the Vienna Genesis purple as orchil, with micro-spectrofluorimetry giving the closest match to *Lasallia pustulata* (*Roccella tinctoria* not excluded) and handheld XRF bromine levels suggestive but which must be regarded with extreme care as evidence against shellfish dyeing (Aceto 2020: Br peak proximity to Hg, washing and degradation losses, and absence of absolute reference values all limit the inference). **Note:** all three studies rely on XRF-no-bromine to exclude murex; Aceto et al. (2015)'s finding of ~100–200 mg/kg bromine in orchil raw material means the logic is unidirectional — bromine *absence* (or very low levels) excludes murex, but quantitative comparison against the orchil-plus-terrestrial background is now the proper discriminator, not strict presence/absence. This reframes the Byzantine-codex literature: prestige purple parchment at the top end of manuscript production was routinely dyed with lichen, not murex. The specific social register varies — the Aceto 2020 chapter describes the Vienna Genesis as "probably custom-made for a private owner rather than for liturgical use" — so claims about imperial patronage of orchil should be made per-manuscript rather than as a blanket generalization. Reinhold's (1969, 1970) framework of purple-as-status-symbol anticipated cheap imitation purples at lower social registers (Reinhold 1969 fn. 17 already cites Caley's 1927 edition of the same Stockholm Papyrus that Whitworth 2025 later reconstructs); the new finding is that the *prestige* end of the purple economy also embraced lichen dye in its most celebrated parchment artifacts. Note that this paradox concerns **parchment** — a substrate distinct from the **wool** Pliny and Dioscorides discuss for *phycos/fucus* textile dyeing and the **pigment** Pliny describes at NH 35.44–46 for *purpurissum*; orchil's presence on parchment is analytically independent of its claimed presence in Roman-era textiles.

### Late-Roman and Byzantine recipe evidence

Whitworth et al. (2025) directly reconstruct Stockholm Papyrus recipe S109 (3rd–4th c. CE), which calls for goat milk in the orchil dyebath and labels the result "Phoenician colour" — an explicit equation of lichen purple with the prestige register of Tyrian dye. The milk increases α-aminoorcein and α-hydroxyorcein uptake several-fold, yielding a deeper, redder hue. Kok (1966) traces orchil continuity from the Florentine medieval rediscovery onward; Whitworth & Koren (2016) document that orchil commercial production continued through the Leeds Bedford firm into the 20th century; the co-presence of a shellfish purple sample in the same archive reflects the Bedfords' scientific interest in ancient purple, not a commercial orchil/murex partnership — the paper explicitly states that "molluscan purple had no direct connection to the lichen trade or other products manufactured by the Bedfords" (p. 266).

### Preservation and detection challenges

Orchil chromophores photodegrade severely: β- and γ-orcein, the purple-hue components, are the most labile (Roca et al. 2026, preprint not yet peer-reviewed). Grierson et al. (1985a) measured ISO Blue Wool lightfastness grades of 1–2 for lichen purples — extremely poor. This means many Roman-period lichen purples have faded beyond visual and analytical detection even when originally present in quantity; the Vindolanda identification depended on survival in waterlogged, anaerobic conditions atypical of most Roman sites (Wild 2011). Non-invasive FORS and spectrofluorimetry (Clementi 2006; Aceto 2015) are now enabling tentative identifications before sampling, improving the odds of future detections.

## Terminology Glossary

Selected terms drawn from the stage-4 summary files across the orchil corpus.

**Orcein** — the crude mixture of purple-red phenoxazone-based pigments produced from orchil lichens by ammonia fermentation; 14 characterised components (8 major + 6 minor; Beecken et al. 1961/2003; Musso 1960).

**Orchil (archil, orseille)** — the finished purple-red textile dye derived from *Roccella* and related lichens by ammonia fermentation; the term encompasses the dye liquor, paste, or dried powder.

**α/β/γ-Hydroxyorcein** — the three structural isomers of the 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone series within orcein; diagnostic markers for HPLC-MS/MS identification (Witkowski et al. 2017).

**α/β/γ-Aminoorcein** — the three structural isomers of the 7-amino-2-phenoxazone series within orcein; together with the hydroxy series constitute the primary analytical fingerprint (Witkowski et al. 2017).

**7-Hydroxy-2-phenoxazone** — core chromophore scaffold of the hydroxyorcein series and of litmus; the fundamental ring system elucidated by Musso (1960).

**7-Amino-2-phenoxazone** — core chromophore scaffold of the aminoorcein series; alternative phenoxazone framework within orcein.

**Lecanoric acid** — orcinol depside (orcinol + orsellinic acid ester); a principal depside in *Roccella* and related lichens (Huneck and Yoshimura 1996), hydrolysed to orcinol during orchil fermentation.

**Erythrin** — orcinol depside glycoside found prominently in *Roccella tinctoria* (confirmed by Frezza et al. 2024 at Ostia Antica); on hydrolysis yields erythritol and lecanoric acid, precursors to orcein.

**Gyrophoric acid** — orcinol tridepside abundant in many lichen species (Huneck and Yoshimura 1996); a further precursor class for orcein formation.

**Depside** — natural ester formed between two phenolic acid units (here orsellinic acid derivatives); the characteristic secondary-metabolite class of orchil lichens (Huneck and Yoshimura 1996), colourless until fermented.

**Orcinol (3,5-dihydroxytoluene)** — the key phenolic monomer released by hydrolysis of depsides; oxidised and ammoniated to yield orcein chromophores (Musso 1960; Beecken et al. 1961/2003).

***Roccella tinctoria*** — the primary Mediterranean orchil lichen; fruticose, maritime rock species; historically harvested from Italian and Iberian coasts, Macaronesia, and North Africa (Kok 1966; Cardon 2007; Frezza et al. 2024).

***R. phycopsis* (= *R. fucoides*)** — second major Mediterranean orchil lichen; *phycopsis* is the correct name per Tehler (2003); all pre-2003 literature using *fucoides* refers to this species.

***R. fuciformis*** — a further *Roccella* species used in orcein synthesis experiments (Roca et al. 2026).

***Ochrolechia tartarea*** — crustose rock lichen; Northern European orchil/cudbear source; yields a bluer hue than *Roccella*; used in Scotland as korkir/corkir (Grierson 1986; Grierson et al. 1985b).

***Lasallia pustulata*** — foliose lichen; secondary orchil source; identified as the *most likely* lichen source in the Vienna Genesis per Aceto et al. 2020 (closest micro-spectrofluorimetry match; *Roccella tinctoria* not excluded) and in English/Central European medieval artifacts (Calà et al. 2019).

***Lecanora sulphurata*** — Mediterranean crustose lichen producing chlorinated xanthones rather than orcein; consistent with the chemosyndrome that Lackner et al. (2024) detected on the early-15th-c. Met Cloisters *Julius Caesar* tapestry (a methodological precedent for non-orcein lichen dyes on historical textiles, not a Roman-period identification). Species assignment is hedged in the original paper; the detected compounds lacked the atranorin/chloroatranorin depsides of fresh *L. sulphurata* (photodegradation).

**Litmus** — a polymeric assembly of 7-hydroxy-2-phenoxazone units derived from the same orchil lichens (Beecken et al. 1961/2003); pH indicator; blue in alkali, red in acid; produced in the Netherlands from the Middle Ages (Kok 1966).

**Cudbear (korkir)** — dried, powdered orchil-type dye from *Ochrolechia tartarea*, patented by Cuthbert Gordon in Scotland c. 1758 (Kok 1966); the Scottish Gaelic equivalent of orchil (Grierson 1986).

**Folium** — purple dye from *Chrozophora tinctoria* (a plant, not a lichen); documented alongside orchil in purple codices by Aceto et al. (2019); previously of undetermined composition.

**Tyrian purple (for contrast)** — shellfish-derived purple from hypobranchial glands of *Hexaplex trunculus*, *Bolinus brandaris*, and *Stramonita haemastoma*; chromophore 6,6'-dibromoindigo (DBI), established by Friedländer (1909); identified in historical samples by HPLC via brominated indigoid markers. Note: Whitworth & Koren (2016) apply established HPLC methods to the Bedford archive sample and explicitly exclude *H. trunculus* as the source of that specific sample ("*H. trunculus* can be ruled out," p. 262); the three-species list here gives the general trade range, not the identification from that sample.

***Argaman* (for contrast)** — Biblical Hebrew term for reddish-purple, associated with molluscan dye; distinguished from *tekhelet* (blue-purple) (Whitworth and Koren 2016). Note: Whitworth & Koren 2016 fn. 7 specifically refutes the claim (Kok 1966) that Ezekiel 27:7 refers to orchil or lichen dyes; both *argaman* and *tekhelet* are shellfish-dye terms in Hebrew sacred and commercial contexts.

***Purpura alia* / *purpura hysginum*** — Roman terms for "other purple" / "woad-purple" (Latin); used in ancient sources and by modern scholars as a label for non-murex purple substitutes; the chemical equivalents of which include orchil (Taylor 1983, tentative single-sample identification), madder+indigo combinations (Wouters et al. 2008; De Moor et al. 2010), and folium (Aceto et al. 2019). (Note: Wouters et al. 2008 establishes the chemical composition of Roman-Egyptian purple as predominantly madder-over-indigoid; the equation of that composition with the Latin terms *purpura alia* / *purpura hysginum* is this survey's editorial inference, not a claim the paper itself makes. The paper does not use either Latin term as an analytical conclusion.)

***Phycos thalassion* / *fucus*** — Greek and Latin terms used by Dioscorides and Pliny for a coastal plant or lichen used in purple dyeing; debated identification with *Roccella*-type lichens, endorsed (not critically argued) by Kok 1966 and Smith 1921; the detailed philological case lives in Whitworth & Koren 2016.

**HPLC-ESI-MS/MS** — high-performance liquid chromatography with electrospray ionization and tandem mass spectrometry; the sensitive method of choice for orcein identification (Witkowski et al. 2017 — the first LC-MS/MS method covering all major orcein components) and lichen-species discrimination (Calà et al. 2019).

**FORS (fibre optic reflectance spectroscopy)** — non-invasive UV-visible diffuse reflectance technique; orchil shows characteristic absorption maxima at ~542 and ~586 nm (Clementi 2006; Aceto et al. 2015, 2020); enables tentative identification before sampling.

**SERS (surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy)** — Raman microscopy on silver colloid particles; provides molecular fingerprints of orchil chromophores and enables tentative species discrimination (Doherty et al. 2014 frame it as "spectral differences possibly owing to their specific dye-precursor constituents" — suggestive rather than hardened).

**SRM transitions (Selected Reaction Monitoring; also marketed as MRM by AB Sciex)** — targeted mass spectrometry transitions (parent → product ion mass pairs) used to identify and quantify specific orcein isomers in historical textiles; the technique is labelled "SRM" in Witkowski et al. (2017), who used a QTRAP 3200 (Applied Biosystems/MDS SCIEX) instrument; AB Sciex instruments use the equivalent commercial label "MRM," which has propagated in the dye-analysis literature as a synonym.

**Ammonia fermentation method (AFM)** — the traditional orchil process: lichen thallus steeped in ammonia solution with aeration, converting depsides to orcein; contrasted with the boiling-water method (BWM) for xanthone-yielding lichens (Lackner et al. 2024).
